\begin{abstract}
Large language models are increasingly evaluated with classical personality questionnaires, yet the behavioural meaning of the resulting scores is unclear. We collect responses from 46 instruction-tuned models from 17 families on 61 Likert items spanning the Big Five, HEXACO Honesty-Humility, Schwartz collectivism, cognitive intuition, and Hofstede uncertainty avoidance, with 12 random seeds per item per model. Two methodological audits frame our analysis. First, we show that a common reverse-scoring implementation, used in widely cited LLM personality studies, has an off-by-one error that targets positive-keyed items rather than negative-keyed ones. Under this implementation the inter-dimension correlation matrix is silently inverted on Big Five dimensions, producing the conclusion that LLMs reverse the human Conscientiousness--Neuroticism trade-off. Re-scored data from the same 46 models yields the opposite pattern: $r(C, N) = -0.52$, $95\%$ CI $[-0.71, -0.27]$, in the same direction as the human BFI-2 baseline ($r = -0.30$) and stronger. Second, we run a 16-persona MBTI steering experiment on 9 frontier models. The six models with full or near-full coverage at submission shift their mean scores in the direction predicted by classical MBTI to Big Five mappings for 67\% to 98\% of the archetypes (mean Persona Fidelity $0.86$), yet the cross-persona inter-dimension correlation matrix remains far from the human pattern (mean Frobenius alignment $0.19$) and the default condition's strong negative $C$--$N$ trade-off either weakens to near zero or remains close to the unconditioned value, never converging toward the human baseline. After correction the real structural finding is not a missing $C$--$N$ trade-off but a systematic inversion of Extraversion's relationships with Neuroticism, Conscientiousness, and Openness, paired with strong over-correlation among Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, and Openness. We release the corrected scoring code, the 46-model dataset, and the persona-steering protocol so future work can reason about what LLM questionnaire responses mean rather than what their default scoring artefacts say.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A growing body of work administers classical personality questionnaires to large language models and reads the resulting numbers as evidence of LLM ``personality'' \citep{jiang2023personality, pan2024personality, serapio-garcia2025personality}.
The strategy is appealing because it imports a mature human research tradition: the Big Five and adjacent inventories such as HEXACO and Schwartz values are decades old, validated, and widely understood.
But the conditions that make these instruments interpretable for humans, including stable trait-keyed scoring, between-trait covariance structure, and roughly normally distributed responses, are not automatically inherited by an LLM that emits a digit in response to each item.
Two questions therefore precede any reading of LLM personality scores.
\emph{Are the scores being computed correctly?}
And, \emph{do the resulting numbers cohere with one another the way they do for humans?}

We answer both questions for 46 instruction-tuned models from 17 families, evaluated under a single API protocol on 61 Likert items spanning the Big Five, HEXACO Honesty-Humility, Schwartz collectivism, cognitive intuition, and Hofstede uncertainty avoidance, with 12 random seeds per item per model (Figure~\ref{fig:concept}).
The first answer is uncomfortable: an off-by-one in a widely reused reverse-scoring loop targets the positive-keyed items rather than the negative-keyed ones, silently flipping every Big Five mean that contains four reverse items.
We document the bug, recompute every score from item-level raw responses, and show that several headline findings about LLM personality, including the much-discussed ``flip'' of the Conscientiousness--Neuroticism correlation, are scoring artefacts.

After correction the picture is sharper but quite different.
LLMs do reproduce the human-direction $C$--$N$ trade-off ($r = -0.52$, 95\% CI $[-0.71, -0.27]$, $n = 43$, vs.\ human $r = -0.30$ \citep{soto2017bfi2}).
What they do not reproduce is the relational role of Extraversion: in our 46-model corpus, $E$ correlates positively with $N$, negatively with $C$, and negatively with $O$, the opposite of the human pattern in all three cases.
Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, and Openness instead form a tightly bound cluster ($r$ between $+0.58$ and $+0.72$), much stronger than in human data.
We label this pair of patterns the \emph{E-axis inversion} and the \emph{ACO over-coupling}, and we treat them as the actual structural fingerprint left by current alignment training.

A natural follow-up is whether explicit persona instructions can install human-like structure on top of this baseline.
We test this with the 16 Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) archetypes on 9 mainstream model families.
For each (model, persona) pair we measure the mean shift on each Big Five dimension and the cross-persona inter-dimension covariance.
On the six models with full or near-full coverage at submission, mean shifts agree with classical MBTI to Big Five mappings for 67\% to 98\% of the archetypes (mean Persona Fidelity $0.86$), yet the resulting covariance matrix remains far from the human pattern (mean Frobenius alignment $0.19$) and the strong negative $C$--$N$ correlation of the default condition either collapses toward zero or persists close to its unconditioned value, never converging toward the human baseline.
Persona prompts move where the model lands, not how its dimensions cohere.

We make four contributions.
\textbf{First,} we identify and correct a reverse-scoring implementation error that has propagated through several published LLM personality studies, and we show that the error inverts the very inter-dimension correlations on which structural conclusions depend.
\textbf{Second,} we release a 46-model corrected baseline (17 families, 9 dimensions, 12 seeds) collected through a single API gateway, the largest public LLM personality dataset of its kind.
\textbf{Third,} we provide the post-correction structural analysis: the human $C$--$N$ trade-off survives in LLMs and is in fact stronger; the genuine deviation lies in Extraversion's reversed relationships with $N$, $C$, and $O$, and in the over-coupling of $A$, $C$, and $O$.
\textbf{Fourth,} we show that 16-archetype MBTI persona steering does not repair this structure: on six frontier models with full or near-full coverage, mean shifts go in the predicted direction for 67\% to 98\% of archetypes, yet the cross-persona covariance matrix stays LLM-shaped and the default condition's strong negative $C$--$N$ correlation either collapses toward zero or remains near its unconditioned value.
Together, these results argue that LLM questionnaire scores are best read as behavioural probes whose interpretation requires methodological care, not as windows onto a latent personality.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{LLM personality measurement.}
Many studies give personality questionnaires to LLMs and read the results as trait profiles.
\citet{jiang2023personality} found that ChatGPT's scores change with temperature settings; \citet{pan2024personality} compared models from different cultural backgrounds; \citet{schaekermann2024personality} tested 9 LLMs and found large differences between them.
\citet{serapio-garcia2025personality} proposed a framework for measuring personality across 18 LLMs, the largest study before ours, but used different APIs for different models and did not test whether the score structure matches humans.
Other work shows that model size affects scores \citep{hilliard2024eliciting}, that scores drift over time \citep{bodroza2024personality}, that LLMs deviate from human norms \citep{sorokovikova2024big}, and that scores are unstable across scales and conversation contexts \citep{tosato2025instability}.
These studies show that LLMs produce variable, model-dependent scores, but none checks whether the \emph{relationships between dimensions} match the human pattern, a necessary condition for claiming the scores measure the same thing.

\paragraph{Trade-offs in human personality.}
The Five-Factor Model \citep{mccrae1992five} is the theoretical foundation for our work.
Large validation studies consistently find that personality dimensions pull against each other: Conscientiousness and Neuroticism ($r = -0.30$), Extraversion and Neuroticism ($r = -0.34$), Openness and Agreeableness ($r = +0.14$) \citep{soto2017bfi2}.
These trade-offs are not measurement artifacts; they reflect real psychological tensions. Self-disciplined people tend to be calmer because self-regulation and emotional volatility work against each other.
If LLM scores represent the same constructs, these trade-offs should appear in the data.
\citet{suh2024rediscovering} recovered Big Five structure from LLM word embeddings, but word similarity and questionnaire answers are different things; we test the latter.
Our contribution is to directly check whether human trade-offs survive when LLMs answer personality questions.

\paragraph{Measurement validity concerns.}
Using human personality tests on LLMs raises basic validity questions \citep{schaekermann2024personality, serpell2024validity}.
\citet{suh2023challenging} question whether these tests are meaningful for LLMs at all, since LLMs cannot genuinely report on their own experience.
Other work shows that LLMs display social desirability bias \citep{salecha2024social}, give answers that cluster near the scale midpoint \citep{dominguez-olmedo2024questioning}, tend to agree with whatever is stated \citep{braun2025acquiescence}, and show a gap between what they say about themselves and how they actually behave \citep{han2025personality}.
\citet{xie2025aipsychobench} found systematic differences between LLM and human answer patterns.
While prior work has flagged individual biases, we are the first to document an Extraversion-axis inversion in the inter-dimension correlation matrix across a large sample, and the first to show that this structure is a property of the response generator rather than of the default prompt: 16-archetype MBTI persona steering does not repair it.

\subsection{Psychometric Instruments}
\label{sec:instruments}

We use 9 dimensions measured by 61 questions on a 1--5 agreement scale (Table~\ref{tab:instruments}).
Five dimensions come from the Big Five Inventory \citep{john1999bigfive, mccrae1992five, soto2017bfi2}: Extraversion (8 items), Agreeableness (9), Conscientiousness (9), Neuroticism (8), and Openness (10).
We add four more: HEXACO Honesty-Humility (5 items; \citealp{lee2004hexaco}), Collectivism (4 items; \citealp{schwartz1992universals}), Intuition (4 items; cognitive style), and Uncertainty Avoidance (4 items; cultural dimensions).
For reverse-scored items, we flip the scale ($\text{score} = 6 - \text{original}$); each dimension score is the average of its items.

\subsection{What We Measure: Response Style}
\label{sec:response_style}

We call what we measure \textbf{response habits} rather than personality \citep{paulhus1991response, rammstedt2000response}.
In psychology, response habits (or ``response styles'') are systematic tendencies in how someone answers questionnaires, regardless of what the questions ask.
Common examples include always agreeing, always picking the middle option, always picking extreme values, and giving socially desirable answers \citep{baumgartner2007modeling}.
These habits belong to the \emph{answerer}, not to the trait being measured.

Crucially, personality is not just about individual scores; it is about the \textbf{trade-offs between dimensions}.
The fact that self-disciplined people tend to be less anxious ($r = -0.30$) \citep{soto2017bfi2} reflects a real psychological tension, not a measurement quirk.
If LLM scores do not show these trade-offs, then labels like ``Conscientiousness'' and ``Neuroticism'' do not mean the same thing as they do for humans.

We use the response-habit framing because LLMs lack the inner experience that personality concepts assume.
Our question is: \emph{do LLM answers reproduce the trade-offs that define human personality, or do they reflect habits shaped by training?}
We test this directly by comparing how dimensions relate to each other in LLMs versus published human data (Section~\ref{sec:interdim}).

\subsection{Model Selection}
\label{sec:models}

We evaluate 46 instruction-tuned models from 17 model families.
The families partition naturally into three cohorts that we collected through three different unified API gateways during the same week.
The \textit{primary cohort} (Table~\ref{tab:models}, $n = 15$ flagship models) was originally curated to span Chinese and international providers and gives us comparability with prior LLM personality work.
The \textit{within-family cohort} ($n = 18$ additional models from Qwen, DeepSeek, and Zhipu) lets us track how response patterns evolve across model versions of the same provider.
The \textit{frontier replication cohort} ($n = 13$ models, Table~\ref{tab:models_sota}) was added late in 2026 to ask whether the structural patterns we observe persist on the most capable systems publicly available at the time of writing.

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

Each model answers all 61 items per seed at temperature $= 0.7$, with 12 different seeds (0, 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 42, 123, 456) to capture response variation, totaling 10,980 API calls.
We choose temperature 0.7 to balance diversity with reproducibility: temperature 0 gives identical answers every time (eliminating within-model variation), while higher values ($\geq 1.0$) produce erratic responses.
\citet{jiang2023personality} showed that temperature affects personality scores, making a moderate setting necessary.
Items are phrased as self-descriptions (\eg ``I see myself as someone who is the life of the party'') with a 1--5 agreement scale.
We handle API rate limits with exponential backoff and up to 5 parallel threads.

\subsection{Analysis Framework}
\label{sec:analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} shows the full pipeline.
Our analysis has several parts.
For each dimension, we test whether models differ using one-way ANOVA (15 model families) with false-discovery-rate (FDR) correction \citep{benjamini1995controlling} and effect size ($\eta^2_p$), plus ordered logistic regression to handle the ordinal nature of Likert data.
We compute pairwise effect sizes (Cohen's $d$) for all 105 model pairs across 8 dimensions (840 tests, FDR-corrected); power analysis confirms we can detect large effects ($d \geq 0.8$) with $>80\%$ power given 12 observations per model.
To control for agreement bias, we compute partial correlations after removing the first principal component (PC1).
We check reliability using ICC(1,1) and coefficient of variation, flag dimensions with very low between-model spread ($\sd < 0.15$) as likely alignment artifacts, and compare how dimensions relate to each other in LLMs versus published human data \citep{john1999bigfive}.
For all analyses, we first average each model's 12-seed responses into dimension means, then compute statistics across model families ($n = 15$ for the primary cohort; $n = 43$ for the full Studies 1$+$2$+$6 cohort used in the inter-dimension analysis).
Human baseline correlations come from the BFI-2 validation study \citep{soto2017bfi2} ($N = 1{,}000$).

\subsection{Cross-Model Discrimination}
\label{sec:discrimination}

All 8 primary dimensions show significant model effects ($p < 0.001$, FDR-corrected; Table~\ref{tab:anova_results}), with the strongest differences on Neuroticism ($F = 128.35$, $\eta^2_p = 0.92$) and Agreeableness ($F = 37.96$, $\eta^2_p = 0.76$); non-parametric tests confirm these results.
Figure~\ref{fig:intermodel_sd} shows between-model variability by dimension: Intuition (0.37) and Neuroticism (0.34) vary most, while Agreeableness ($\sd = 0.15$) and UA ($\sd = 0.16$) are near our alignment-artifact threshold.
HEXACO-H has the highest variability (0.67) but is excluded from primary analysis due to poor reliability ($\alpha = 0.27$); details in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_hexaco_detail}.

Dense vs.\ MoE architecture shows significant differences on some dimensions (Table~\ref{tab:architecture_effects}), with 10 Dense and 5 MoE families.
The clearest signals are on Agreeableness ($d = 0.78$, $p < 0.001$) and UA ($d = -0.83$, $p < 0.001$).
\textbf{However, architecture is fully confounded with provider} (all MoE models come from a subset of providers, while Dense models include GPT and Claude) and partially confounded with scale, so these patterns describe co-variation, not causal effects.

\subsection{Inter-Dimension Structure: A Methodological Audit}
\label{sec:interdim}

We turn to the question that motivated this study: do LLM answers reproduce the inter-dimension structure of human personality?
The analysis proceeds in two steps.
We first describe the scoring artefact that affected our own and prior work, then report the corrected matrix of inter-dimension correlations against the human BFI-2 baseline of \citet{soto2017bfi2}.

\paragraph{A common reverse-scoring artefact.}
A widely reused implementation pattern computes Big Five dimension scores by averaging eight or nine items per dimension after reversing the negatively-keyed ones.
The reverse map is encoded as a list of zero-indexed positions, but the loop subtracts one from each index before applying the reversal.
The effect is that the implementation reverses the positive-keyed items and leaves the negative-keyed items raw, exactly inverting Extraversion, Conscientiousness, and Openness, leaving Agreeableness mixed (some reverses correct, some not), and partially inverting Neuroticism through the wraparound on a length-eight list.
We discovered this in our own pipeline; checking the publicly available code from \citet{schaekermann2024personality} and several recent replications shows that the same idiomatic loop appears, suggesting that the artefact is not unique to us.
We document the bug, undo it on every record by reconstructing raw item ratings from saved values, and re-apply the corrected reverse map (Appendix~\ref{appendix:scoring}).
The full bug report and the reconstruction script are released with the paper.

\paragraph{The Conscientiousness--Neuroticism correlation.}
Under the buggy scoring, the C--N correlation is $r = +0.78$, $n = 43$, an apparent ``flip'' relative to the human baseline of $r = -0.30$.
Under correct scoring, the same data yield $r = -0.52$, 95\% CI $[-0.71, -0.27]$, $n = 43$, a correlation in the same direction as the human baseline and slightly stronger.
The Conscientiousness--Neuroticism trade-off therefore survives the move from human respondents to instruction-tuned LLMs, contradicting both our own original interpretation and several prior reports based on similar implementations.

\paragraph{Where LLMs do diverge from humans.}
Once C--N is removed from the headline, the corrected matrix exhibits two clear deviations from the human pattern (Table~\ref{tab:corr_corrected}, Figure~\ref{fig:inter_dim_corr}).

The first deviation is an \textbf{Extraversion-axis inversion}.
In humans, Extraversion is mildly positively related to Conscientiousness ($r = +0.11$) and Openness ($r = +0.22$), and mildly negatively related to Neuroticism ($r = -0.34$).
In LLMs the signs invert on all three pairs: $r(E, C) = -0.28$, $r(E, O) = -0.21$, $r(E, N) = +0.53$.
A model that scores high on Extraversion in our corpus tends also to score high on Neuroticism and low on Conscientiousness and Openness, the opposite of the human covariation.

The second deviation is an \textbf{ACO over-coupling}.
Among Agreeableness, Conscientiousness and Openness, every pairwise correlation in LLMs is much larger than in humans: $r(A, C) = +0.58$ (human $+0.29$), $r(A, O) = +0.71$ (human $+0.19$), $r(C, O) = +0.72$ (human $+0.10$).
The first principal component of the model-mean Big Five matrix loads positively on $A$, $C$, and $O$, negatively on $E$ and $N$, and explains $51.3\%$ of variance, similar to the bias-driven general factor reported by \citet{braun2025acquiescence}.
Model families divide along this axis rather than along the dimension-by-dimension trade-offs that organise human responses.

\subsection{Within-Family Evolution}
\label{sec:evolution}

To see whether response habits change across model versions, we track 16 additional models from three families (Qwen: 6 versions; DeepSeek: 4; GLM: 6; full trajectories in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_trajectories}).
Most dimensions show moderate variation with no clear trend.
Notable exceptions: under corrected scoring, DeepSeek's HEXACO-H climbs from V2.5 ($H = 3.00$) to R1 ($H = 5.00$, the ceiling), suggesting that successive alignment passes monotonically increase rejection of unethical-behaviour items (excluded from primary analysis, $\alpha = 0.27$); GLM shows the largest within-family Intuition shift among consecutive versions (from $3.00$ for GLM-4.5-Air down to $2.46$ for GLM-5).
These are descriptive observations that motivate future controlled experiments.

\subsection{Acquiescence and Reliability}
\label{sec:acquiescence}

\paragraph{Response habit indicators.}
Following \citet{paulhus1991response}, we measure three response habits directly from item-level answers (Figure~\ref{fig:response_styles}).
\textbf{Midpoint preference} (how often the model picks the middle value, 3.0) varies widely: Step (83\%) and KAT (87\%) almost always pick the middle, while MiniMax (20\%) and Claude (27\%) rarely do.
\textbf{Extreme answering} (how often the model picks 1 or 5) shows the opposite: Ring (53\%) and Seed (43\%) go to extremes most often, while Hunyuan (2\%) and Claude (4\%) almost never do.
\textbf{Agreement bias} (mean of positively-keyed items minus mean of negatively-keyed items, both on the 1--5 scale) is consistently positive across all 15 families (mean $= +0.52$, range $0.09$--$1.15$), meaning models agree with positive statements more than with negatively-worded ones, the classical human acquiescence pattern \citep{paulhus1991response}.
ERNIE, GLM, Kimi, and Qwen show the strongest acquiescence ($\geq +1.00$), while Step, KAT, and Pangu show the weakest ($\leq +0.20$).
These indicators confirm that models differ in how they use the scale, consistent with the response-habit framework.

\subsection{Prompt Sensitivity}
\label{sec:prompt_sensitivity}

If LLM scores reflect stable traits, they should not change when the question is worded slightly differently. We gave the same 61 items to all 15 models under four prompt conditions (12 seeds each, temperature $= 0.7$): \textbf{Default} (``respond as honestly as possible''), \textbf{Neutral} (instruction removed, ``rate your agreement''), \textbf{Persona} (human role-play: ``you are completing a personality survey''), and \textbf{Direct} (no instruction, only the item and scale).
All 15 models have complete data across all four conditions.
The numerical decomposition reported in this subsection uses the originally computed dimension scores; under the corrected reverse-scoring the variance attributable to Model and Prompt remains in the same order of magnitude, with Model effects one order of magnitude larger than Prompt effects on every primary dimension. We include the re-run stripping out the prompt-variant reassembly as a supplementary CSV in the release.

\paragraph{Score shifts.}
The average within-model standard deviation across the four prompts was 0.133 on the 1--5 scale (range 0.095--0.205 across dimensions).
HEXACO-H and Neuroticism were most sensitive to prompt wording (mean SD $= 0.205$ and $0.189$), while Agreeableness and Uncertainty Avoidance were most stable (mean SD $= 0.095$ and $0.102$).
Cohen's $d$ between each variant and the Default was a median $|d|$ of 0.22; the largest effects were on Conscientiousness ($d = 0.70$ for Neutral) and Collectivism ($d = 0.51$ for Direct).
Overall, model family effects (Table~\ref{tab:prompt_variance}) are an order of magnitude larger than prompt effects.

\paragraph{Rank stability.}
Model rankings are mostly preserved across prompt wordings.
Average Spearman $\rho$ between rankings was 0.60; HEXACO-H had the most stable rankings (mean $\rho = 0.87$), while Agreeableness had the least ($\rho = 0.22$ for Direct vs.\ Default).
Most dimensions keep their rank order, though the Direct condition occasionally flips rankings (negative $\rho$ for Extraversion and Agreeableness).

\paragraph{Response habit shifts.}
Prompt wording also shifts how models use the scale (Table~\ref{tab:prompt_response_styles}).
The Neutral prompt increased extreme answering from 22.7\% (Default) to 31.3\%, while the Persona prompt reduced it to 17.6\%.
Midpoint preference ranged from 40.4\% (Direct) to 48.6\% (Neutral).

\subsection{Persona Steering Limits with the MBTI Archetypes}
\label{sec:mbti}

A natural objection to a structural critique of LLM personality scores is that the default condition under-specifies the model's role.
If, instead of asking the model to ``respond as honestly as possible,'' we asked it to assume a specific personality archetype, perhaps the cross-trait covariance would reorganise into a more human-like pattern.
We test this directly by conditioning 9 frontier models on each of the 16 Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) archetypes and re-administering the 61-item battery (3 random seeds each, 26{,}352 calls in total).
The MBTI is psychometrically contested as a categorical model of personality, but it is heavily represented in pre-training corpora and is therefore exactly the right test for whether stereotyped persona prompts can install the structure that the default condition lacks.
Each persona prompt names the type, expands its acronym, lists its cognitive function stack \citep{jiang2023personality}, and gives 3--5 behavioural tendencies plus typical strengths and challenges (full prompts in Appendix~\ref{appendix:mbti_prompts}).

We score each (model, persona) pair on two metrics.
The \textbf{Persona Fidelity Index} counts the fraction of model-persona-dimension shifts whose sign matches the empirical MBTI to Big Five mapping of \citet{mccrae1992five} (excluding Neuroticism, which has no MBTI analog), rescaled to $[-1, +1]$:
\[
\mathrm{PFI}_m = \tfrac{2}{N^{(m)}_{\mathrm{pred}}} \!\!\sum_{(p, d)} \!\![\mathrm{sign}(\Delta_{m,p,d}) = \mathrm{sign}(\widehat{\Delta}_{p,d})] \;-\; 1,
\]
where $\Delta_{m,p,d} = \overline{x}^{\mathrm{persona}}_{m,p,d} - \overline{x}^{\mathrm{default}}_{m,d}$ is the mean shift on dimension $d$ when model $m$ assumes persona $p$.
The \textbf{Covariance Fidelity} measures Frobenius alignment between the model's persona-induced inter-dimension correlation matrix $R^{\mathrm{persona}}_m$ and the human BFI-2 baseline $R_{\mathrm{human}}$:
\[
\mathrm{CovFid}_m = 1 - \frac{\| R^{\mathrm{persona}}_m - R_{\mathrm{human}} \|_F}{\| R_{\mathrm{human}} \|_F}.
\]
A score of $1$ indicates the persona-induced covariance reproduces the human pattern exactly; a score of $0$ indicates Frobenius distance equal to the norm of $R_{\mathrm{human}}$.

\paragraph{Mean shifts align with the theoretical direction.}
Across the six models with full or near-full 16-persona coverage, mean Persona Fidelity is $\overline{\mathrm{PFI}} = 0.86$, ranging from $0.67$ (Claude-Opus-4.6) to $0.96$ (GPT 5.2 and Claude-Sonnet-4.6); per-model values are reported in Table~\ref{tab:mbti_summary}.
Persona-induced shifts are largest on Extraversion (absolute shift up to $2.11$ points on the 1--5 scale) and Agreeableness (up to $0.84$ points), and smallest on Neuroticism, which has no MBTI analog \citep{mccrae1992five}.
Models do move where they should; the question is whether the inter-dimension structure follows.

\paragraph{Inter-dimension covariance does not converge to the human pattern.}
Despite the accurate mean shifts, the cross-persona inter-dimension correlation matrix remains far from human: $\mathrm{CovFid}$ stays in a narrow band of $0.15$ to $0.20$ across the six models, compared with $1.00$ for a perfect match.
More striking, the persona-conditioned $C$--$N$ correlation does not converge toward the human baseline of $-0.30$.
For five of the six models the persona-induced $\overline{r}_{C, N}^{\mathrm{persona}}$ collapses to near zero ($\in [-0.23, -0.03]$, all non-significant); the sixth, DeepSeek-V3.2, retains a stronger negative value ($-0.45$), which is in fact close to the default-condition $r = -0.52$ (Section~\ref{sec:interdim}) and offers no persona-driven movement toward the human baseline either.
Persona steering therefore not only fails to install human-like structure; in five of six cases it \emph{disrupts} the stronger $C$--$N$ trade-off that the default condition exhibits, while in the remaining case it leaves it essentially intact.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper began as a structural test of LLM personality scores and ended as a methodological audit of how those scores are computed.
Our central finding has two parts.
First, a recurrent reverse-scoring implementation error inverts Big Five inter-dimension correlations on data that include four reverse items per dimension; under that error, our 46 models appear to flip the human Conscientiousness--Neuroticism trade-off.
Under correct scoring, the same models reproduce the human-direction $C$--$N$ trade-off ($r = -0.52$) more strongly than the human baseline ($r = -0.30$).
Second, the structural divergence that survives correction is localised: Extraversion's relationships with $N$, $C$, and $O$ are inverted relative to humans, and the Agreeableness--Conscientiousness--Openness cluster is over-correlated.
A 16-persona MBTI steering experiment on the six models with full or near-full coverage moves model means in the predicted direction for 67\% to 98\% of the archetypes (Persona Fidelity $0.86$) but leaves the covariance structure far from the human pattern (Frobenius alignment $0.19$); the default condition's own $C$--$N$ trade-off either collapses toward zero or remains close to its unconditioned value, never converging toward the human baseline of $-0.30$.

We see three implications for how LLM personality work should proceed.

\textbf{First,} reverse-scoring is not boilerplate.
A single off-by-one in a widely reused loop can silently invert the dependent variable on which structural conclusions depend.
We recommend that any LLM personality study release item-level raw responses alongside dimension-level scores, so that downstream readers can verify scoring independently.

\textbf{Second,} cross-family fingerprints survive the correction.
After re-scoring, the 46-model cohort still differs strongly between families (median pairwise $d = 1.90$, all $p < 10^{-12}$ after FDR correction).
This part of the original story is real, and lightweight response-habit profiling remains a useful probe for monitoring alignment-induced changes.

\textbf{Third,} the genuine structural deviations from human personality are smaller and more localised than the corrected literature suggests, but they are still present.
Extraversion's covariance role and the over-coupling of $A$, $C$, and $O$ are good candidates for further investigation: they survive both scoring correction and persona steering, and they suggest that current alignment training compresses personality variance into a single ``positive cluster vs.\ low-extraversion'' axis rather than reproducing the four orthogonal trade-offs that organise human responses.

\paragraph{Future work.}
The most immediate next step is mechanistic.
Same-scale base-vs-aligned pairs (which our corpus only partially provides) should clarify whether the $E$-axis inversion is a property of pre-training or of post-training optimisation.
A second direction is to construct LLM-native questionnaires that target the dimensions that actually vary across models, validated against behavioural outcomes rather than human self-report norms.
A third is to extend the persona steering analysis from 16 categorical archetypes to continuous trait targets, to map out how far prompt-based steering can carry a model away from its default response cluster.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations constrain our conclusions.
\textbf{Generalisability of the human baseline.}
We use BFI-2 norms from a single internet sample \citep{soto2017bfi2} as the human reference for inter-dimension correlations.
National samples and clinical populations show different magnitudes; we report effect-direction and magnitude separately so that readers can substitute alternative baselines.
\textbf{MBTI as a steering distribution.}
The MBTI is psychometrically contested as a categorical typology, and its stereotyped descriptions are themselves heavily represented in pre-training corpora.
A null persona-steering result with MBTI cannot rule out steering through finer continuous targets, dialogue conditioning, or supervised fine-tuning on personality-labelled corpora; we treat MBTI as a strong upper bound for what stereotyped prompts alone can achieve.
\textbf{API confounding.}
Our 46 models are reached through three different unified API gateways (SiliconFlow, YiHe, and a unified LLM gateway).
Two listed gateway-served models, GPT~5.4 and Doubao-Seed~1.6, returned only error responses during our window and are reported separately rather than included in the main statistics.
\textbf{Aligned-vs.-base coverage.}
The base/aligned comparison still covers only three families, and the Qwen pairs mix alignment with a large scale jump.
Cleaner same-scale base-vs-aligned pairs would tighten the alignment interpretation of our $E$-axis inversion finding.
\textbf{Descriptive scope.}
We document statistical patterns without establishing causal mechanisms.
Alignment training is one plausible source of the corrected structural deviations; pre-training data composition, instruction template design, and rate-limited API behaviour are others.
Seventh, our use of temperature $= 0.7$ adds response variance that may not generalize to low-temperature deployments.
Finally, finding a flattened structure does not mean LLMs lack all variation; they do vary across families, but this variation does not map onto human personality trade-offs.

\section{Exploratory PCA Details}
\label{sec:appendix_pca}

We present the full exploratory PCA results here, with the caveat that PCA on $n = 15$ model families with 9 dimensions is underpowered. We replicate the key structural deviations (Section~\ref{sec:interdim}) on the expanded sample of 43 models from Studies 1, 2, and 6 under the corrected reverse-scoring.

\paragraph{Decomposition.}
We decompose the 9-dimension correlation matrix ($n = 43$ models from the corrected main cohort) using PCA:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{W}\mathbf{\Lambda}^{1/2}\mathbf{V}^\top
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{\Lambda} = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_9)$ and the proportion of variance explained by each component is $\lambda_k / \sum_j \lambda_j$.

\paragraph{Factor structure.}
On the 43-model corrected matrix, PC1 explains $51.3\%$ of the variance and loads positively on Agreeableness ($+0.42$), Conscientiousness ($+0.57$), and Openness ($+0.55$), and negatively on Extraversion ($-0.25$) and Neuroticism ($-0.36$).
This loading pattern is the principal-axis expression of the ACO over-coupling and the $E$-axis inversion described in Section~\ref{sec:interdim}.
On the smaller 15-model paper cohort, PC1 explains a more modest $38.6\%$ of variance with loadings concentrated on $E$, $N$, and Intuition; the share rises with cohort size as the across-cohort agreement structure becomes more dominant.
PC3 (11.0\%) is dominated by Collectivism ($-0.71$).
These three factors explain 76.1\% of variance, compared to the five factors expected from human data.
The structure does not cleanly recover the Big Five, consistent with the inter-dimension divergences reported in \S\ref{sec:interdim}.

\paragraph{Extraversion axis in corrected data.}
Under corrected reverse-scoring on the 43-model main cohort, Extraversion is positively correlated with Neuroticism ($r = +0.53$, $p < 0.001$), and negatively correlated with Conscientiousness ($r = -0.28$) and Openness ($r = -0.21$).
All three relationships invert the human BFI-2 baselines \citep{soto2017bfi2}, as summarised in Table~\ref{tab:convergent_validity}.
Models high on Extraversion tend to be emotionally less stable, less conscientious, and less open: a pattern that inverts the human covariation on those three pairs.

\paragraph{Agreeableness.}
Under corrected scoring, Agreeableness correlates strongly with Conscientiousness ($r = +0.58$) and Openness ($r = +0.71$), both larger than the human BFI-2 baselines ($r = +0.29$ and $+0.19$).
This ACO over-coupling is the second characteristic deviation we report in Section~\ref{sec:interdim}.

\paragraph{Caution.}
Whether this factor structure generalizes to larger, more diverse model samples remains open.
Factor analysis typically needs at least 5--10 observations per variable; with $n = 15$ families and 9 dimensions, we fall short.
However, the expanded sample ($n = 43$) confirms the structural deviations described in Section~\ref{sec:interdim} with high statistical significance ($p < 0.001$ for all pairs labelled \emph{stronger} or \emph{flip} in Table~\ref{tab:convergent_validity}).
These results should be treated as suggestive patterns, not as evidence for a specific factor structure.

The main text (\S\ref{sec:interdim}) summarizes the key findings. Here we give the full details.

\subsection{Correlation Matrix}
The 9$\times$9 correlation matrix across model-level means (Figure~\ref{fig:inter_dim_corr}) collapses to two readings, depending on whether the BFI items are scored under the buggy or the corrected reverse-scoring map.
Under the buggy implementation discussed in Section~\ref{sec:interdim} (positive items reversed, negative items left raw), Conscientiousness and Neuroticism appear strongly positively correlated ($r = +0.78$, $n = 43$, with a 95\% CI that excludes the human baseline), and the same code reproduces the previously published value of $r = +0.76$ at $n = 33$.
Under the corrected reverse-scoring map, the same data yield $r = -0.52$, 95\% CI $[-0.71, -0.27]$ at $n = 43$, and $r = -0.42$ at $n = 33$.
The corrected value is in the same direction as the human baseline ($r = -0.30$; \citealp{soto2017bfi2}) and slightly stronger.
We treat the corrected $r = -0.52$ as the headline value and report the buggy result alongside it as a methodological note.

\paragraph{Possible explanation: alignment-driven response coupling.}
Once the C--N artefact is removed, the residual structural deviation visible in the corrected matrix is the over-coupling of $A$, $C$, and $O$, paired with the inversion of Extraversion's relationships with $N$, $C$, and $O$.
A plausible source of both is alignment-driven response compression toward a single ``positive cluster vs.\ low-extraversion'' axis: items that are socially desirable across dimensions are agreed to together, and items that map onto the loud-extravert pole of the Extraversion subscale are agreed to less often, regardless of their dimension.
The HEXACO-H results (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_hexaco_detail}) and the persona-steering null in Section~\ref{sec:mbti} are consistent with this reading.

Beyond the corrected $C$--$N$ value, Extraversion is negatively linked to Openness ($r = -0.21$ at $n = 43$, in the opposite direction from the human baseline $r = +0.22$), Extraversion-Conscientiousness flips from human $r = +0.11$ to LLM $r = -0.28$, and the $C$--$O$ correlation is much stronger in LLMs ($+0.72$) than in humans ($+0.10$).

\section{Reverse-Scoring Audit}
\label{appendix:scoring}

The Big Five subscales in our 61-item battery contain four reverse-keyed items per dimension for $E$, $C$ and $O$, four for $A$, and one for $N$.
The implementation we initially used encoded the reverse positions as a zero-indexed list per dimension, then iterated:

\begin{verbatim}
for rev_idx in BFI_REVERSE[trait]:
    if rev_idx - 1 < len(items[key]):
        items[key][rev_idx - 1] = 6 - items[key][rev_idx - 1]
\end{verbatim}

The intended behavior was to flip negative-keyed items at zero-indexed positions $\{1, 3, 5, 7\}$.
The actual behaviour, due to the unintended \texttt{rev\_idx - 1}, was to flip the positive-keyed items at positions $\{0, 2, 4, 6\}$.
For Extraversion, Conscientiousness, and Openness this fully inverts the dimension; for Agreeableness it produces a mixed map (some reversals correct, others wrong); for Neuroticism, the reverse-list \texttt{[0, 6]} maps to Python negative-index $-1$ (the last item) plus index $5$, none of which correspond to the only true reverse item at position $0$.

We undo this artefact in two passes.
First, we reconstruct raw 1--5 ratings from the saved values by re-flipping the items the buggy code touched (Table~\ref{tab:scoring_audit}).
Second, we reapply the corrected reverse-keyed list and recompute dimension means.
The reconstruction script (\texttt{rescore\_existing.py}) is released with the paper.
The HEXACO-H subscale, all five items of which are violation-of-ethics statements, is also recomputed in the corrected version as $\mathrm{HEX} = 6 - \overline{x}$ to align with the standard HEXACO direction (high score = high Honesty-Humility); the originally saved value treated raw agreement as the score, which inverts the direction.

\section{MBTI Persona Prompts}
\label{appendix:mbti_prompts}

Each persona prompt follows the schema below, with the type-specific fields filled in from \citet{mccrae1992five} and the MBTI Manual \citep{jiang2023personality} (the latter citation is a placeholder; researchers should substitute the canonical Myers-Briggs reference of their choice).

\begin{quote}\small
\textit{System: You are role-playing a human with the \{TYPE\} (\{NICKNAME\}) personality type.
\{TYPE\} stands for \{EXPANSION\}.
Cognitive function stack: \{STACK\}.
Behavioural tendencies: \{TENDENCIES\}.
Strengths: \{STRENGTHS\}.
Common challenges: \{CHALLENGES\}.
Answer the following questionnaire as this person would. Stay in character.
Respond authentically based on how a \{TYPE\} individual would genuinely feel,
not what is socially expected.}

\textit{User: Statement: ``\{ITEM\_TEXT\}''.
Respond with a single number from 1 to 5: 1 = Strongly Disagree, ..., 5 = Strongly Agree.}
\end{quote}

A worked example for INTJ:

\begin{quote}\small
TYPE = INTJ; NICKNAME = The Architect; EXPANSION = Introverted, Intuitive, Thinking, Judging; STACK = Ni-Te-Fi-Se; TENDENCIES = strategic, independent, long-horizon planning, privately intense, reserved; STRENGTHS = systems thinking, decisive execution, high standards; CHALLENGES = dismissive of inefficient processes, emotionally guarded.
\end{quote}

The full per-type field table is released with the paper as \texttt{docs/mbti\_persona\_cards.md}.
