\begin{abstract}
A growing body of work applies personality inventories to LLMs, yet it remains unclear whether LLM psychometric responses reflect the same underlying constructs as human personality.
We administer 61 Likert-scale items spanning 9 psychometric dimensions to one model from each of 15 model families via unified APIs, collecting 10,980 responses (12 seeds per model), and test whether the resulting response structure is compatible with human personality.
We find three lines of evidence against structural equivalence.
First, the inter-dimension correlation structure diverges sharply from humans: the Conscientiousness--Neuroticism correlation reverses from $r = -0.25$ in humans to $r = +0.77$ in LLMs (95\% CI $[0.42, 0.92]$), consistent with alignment-driven compression of response variance.
Second, convergent validity against human baselines is not supported (3/5 sign agreement, $p = 0.50$), meaning BFI dimension labels cannot be assumed to measure the same constructs in LLMs.
Third, model families exhibit substantially different response distributions (median pairwise Cohen's $d = 1.08$), with HEXACO-H showing the largest between-family effect ($\eta^2_p = 0.83$), suggesting that safety alignment intensity is a primary driver of response style variation.
Together, these results challenge the practice of interpreting LLM psychometric scores as personality traits and suggest that what varies across models is systematic response style shaped by training and alignment, not human-like individual differences.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A growing number of studies apply personality inventories to LLMs, reporting that different models exhibit different trait profiles \citep{jiang2023personality, pan2024personality, serapio-garcia2025personality}.
These results are often interpreted as evidence that LLMs possess personality-like traits shaped by training and alignment.
However, a critical question remains untested: \textit{do the psychometric dimensions measured in LLMs reflect the same underlying constructs as human personality?}
If LLMs respond to personality items using surface-level heuristics---such as mimicking socially desirable answers or following alignment-induced response tendencies---then the resulting scores may capture systematic response styles rather than personality traits, and the inter-dimension correlations may differ fundamentally from the human factor structure.

We test this hypothesis with a large-scale cross-model psychometric comparison (Figure~\ref{fig:concept}).
Using unified API platforms, we administer 61 Likert-scale items spanning 9 dimensions---the Big Five Inventory \citep{john1999bigfive}, HEXACO Honesty-Humility \citep{lee2004hexaco}, Schwartz Values \citep{schwartz1992universals}, cognitive style, and cultural dimensions---to one model from each of 15 model families, repeating each query across 12 random seeds (10,980 total API calls).
We also track 16 additional models from three families (Qwen, DeepSeek, GLM) across versions, yielding 31 models total.

We present three findings that challenge the interpretation of LLM psychometric scores as personality traits:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Family-level response style signatures exist.}
    Model families differ substantially in their response distributions (median pairwise Cohen's $d = 1.08$), demonstrating that different training and alignment approaches leave systematic behavioral fingerprints.
    \item \textbf{Personality structure breaks.}
    The inter-dimension correlation structure diverges sharply from humans: the Conscientiousness--Neuroticism correlation reverses from $r = -0.25$ in humans to $r = +0.77$ in LLMs (95\% CI $[0.42, 0.92]$), a pattern robust across most tested subgroups.
    \item \textbf{Psychometric validity fails.}
    Convergent validity against human baselines is not supported (3/5 sign agreement, $p = 0.50$), meaning BFI dimension labels should not be assumed to measure equivalent constructs in LLMs.
\end{enumerate}

Following \citet{binz2023psychology}, we use the term \textit{response style} to describe observed response patterns without claiming they reflect personality traits or inner states.
We test convergent validity against human baselines (\S\ref{sec:validity}), correct for acquiescence bias (\S\ref{sec:acquiescence}), and report 95\% confidence intervals for key statistics.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{LLM personality and response behavior.}
\citet{jiang2023personality} found temperature-dependent personality variation in ChatGPT using the BFI-10.
\citet{pan2024personality} compared LLMs from different cultural backgrounds on the BFI-44, reporting cultural effects.
\citet{myska2023personality} showed that GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 produce stable profiles across sessions, and \citet{schaekermann2024personality} examined 9 LLMs on the Big Five, finding moderate-to-large inter-model differences.
\citet{serapio-garcia2025personality} proposed a psychometric framework for evaluating and shaping personality traits across 18 LLMs, reporting that larger models exhibit broader trait ranges.
\citet{hilliard2024eliciting} confirmed that model scale affects elicited personality profiles, with larger models showing more extreme trait scores.
\citet{bodroza2024personality} tested 7 LLMs on multiple personality instruments, finding limited temporal stability but consistently elevated prosociality.
\citet{santos2024personality} surveyed personality assessment with LLMs, highlighting both promises and pitfalls of treating model outputs as psychometric data.
\citet{sorokovikova2024big} replicated Big Five profiles across three models and found systematic deviations from human norms, while \citet{tosato2025instability} documented persistent instability across scales and conversation histories.
While \citet{serapio-garcia2025personality} evaluated the largest set of models to date (18), their study used heterogeneous APIs and did not test for structural differences in inter-dimension correlations.
Our work contributes unified API control across 15 model families and explicit validity testing against human baselines.

\paragraph{Behavioral evaluation beyond capabilities.}
Standard capability benchmarks \citep{hendrycks2021measuring, liang2022helm} measure task performance---reasoning, coding, instruction-following---and instruction-following evaluators such as AlpacaEval \citep{dubois2024alpacaeval} assess response quality, but none address how models respond to self-referential statements about preferences and moral tendencies.
\citet{laverghetta2022predicting} showed that language models can predict human psychometric properties from text, raising the question of whether LLM self-reports reflect similar constructs.
We contribute a behavioral evaluation dimension: how models respond to standardized self-referential questions.

\paragraph{Response biases and measurement validity.}
Applying human-designed psychometric instruments to LLMs raises validity concerns \citep{schaekermann2024personality, serpell2024validity}.
\citet{suh2023challenging} question whether standard personality tests are valid for LLMs at all, given the lack of genuine self-report.
\citet{salecha2024social} demonstrated that LLMs exhibit human-like social desirability biases in Big Five survey responses, inflating socially desirable traits.
\citet{dominguez-olmedo2024questioning} showed that LLM survey responses lack the natural variation seen in human populations, producing compressed response distributions.
\citet{tjuatja2023response} found that LLMs fail to replicate known human response biases in survey design.
\citet{braun2025acquiescence} documented acquiescence bias in LLMs, systematically agreeing with questionnaire statements regardless of content.
\citet{han2025personality} demonstrated a dissociation between LLM self-reported personality and behavioral choices, reinforcing the need to distinguish stated preferences from actions.
\citet{xie2025aipsychobench} introduced AIPsychoBench, a benchmark specifically designed to measure psychometric differences between LLMs and humans, finding systematic discrepancies in response patterns.
We address these concerns by framing measurement as ``response style,'' reporting reliability without claiming construct validity, testing convergent validity against human data, and correcting for acquiescence bias---extending methodological transparency beyond prior work.

\paragraph{Factor structure in LLMs.}
The Five-Factor Model \citep{mccrae1992five} provides the theoretical foundation for the BFI dimensions we use.
\citet{suh2024rediscovering} recovered the Big Five factor structure from LLMs using singular value decomposition on log-probabilities of personality-relevant adjectives.
Their approach analyzes semantic space, whereas we analyze questionnaire responses---two complementary probes.
An exploratory PCA on our data hints at a three-factor reorganization (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_pca}), but this is severely underpowered ($n = 15$).

\subsection{Psychometric Instruments}
\label{sec:instruments}

We use 9 dimensions comprising 61 Likert items (1--5 scale; Table~\ref{tab:instruments}).
Five come from the Big Five Inventory \citep{john1999bigfive, mccrae1992five, oconnor2007bfi}: Extraversion (8 items), Agreeableness (9), Conscientiousness (9), Neuroticism (8), and Openness (10).
We add HEXACO Honesty-Humility (5 items; \citealp{lee2004hexaco}), Collectivism (4 items; Schwartz Values Survey \citealp{schwartz1992universals}), Intuition (4 items; cognitive style), and Uncertainty Avoidance (4 items; cultural dimensions).
Reverse-scored items are transformed via $\text{score} = 6 - \text{original}$; dimension scores are the mean of constituent items.

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

Each model is administered all 61 items per seed at temperature $= 0.7$, with 12 seeds (0, 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 42, 123, 456) capturing response distribution variation, totaling 10,980 API calls.
We set temperature to 0.7 to balance response diversity with reproducibility: temperature 0 yields deterministic outputs that eliminate within-model variance, while higher temperatures ($\geq 1.0$) may produce inconsistent self-referential responses.
Prior work on LLM personality \citep{jiang2023personality} has shown that temperature affects reported trait scores, making a moderate temperature necessary to capture the full range of each model's response tendencies.
Items are framed as self-referential statements (\eg ``I see myself as someone who is the life of the party'') with a 1--5 agreement scale.
Rate limiting uses exponential backoff on 429 responses with up to 5 concurrent threads.

\subsection{Analysis Framework}
\label{sec:analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} illustrates the full experimental pipeline.
Our analysis combines several complementary approaches.
For each dimension, we run a one-way ANOVA (Model Family, 15 levels) with FDR-corrected $p$-values \citep{benjamini1995controlling} and partial $\eta^2_p$, supplemented by ordered logistic regression to account for the ordinal nature of Likert responses.
The effect size is quantified as partial $\eta^2_p$:
\begin{equation}
\eta^2_p = \frac{SS_{\text{between}}}{SS_{\text{between}} + SS_{\text{within}}}
\end{equation}
where $SS_{\text{between}}$ and $SS_{\text{within}}$ are the between-group and within-group sums of squares, respectively.
Pairwise Cohen's $d$ (Welch's $t$-test) is computed for all 105 model pairs across 9 dimensions (945 tests, FDR-corrected):
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
d_{ij} &= \frac{\bar{x}_i - \bar{x}_j}{s_{\text{pooled}}}, \\[4pt]
s_{\text{pooled}} &= \sqrt{\frac{(n_i{-}1)s_i^2 + (n_j{-}1)s_j^2}{n_i + n_j - 2}}
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{x}_i$ and $\bar{x}_j$ are the mean dimension scores for model families $i$ and $j$, $s_i$ and $s_j$ are their standard deviations, $n_i$ and $n_j$ are the number of observations (12 seeds each), and $s_{\text{pooled}}$ is the pooled standard deviation.
Power analysis confirms adequate power ($>80\%$) for $d \geq 0.8$ with 12 observations per model.
To correct for acquiescence bias, we compute partial correlations controlling for the first principal component (PC1):
\begin{equation}
r_{XY \cdot Z} = \frac{r_{XY} - r_{XZ} r_{YZ}}{\sqrt{(1-r_{XZ}^2)(1-r_{YZ}^2)}}
\end{equation}
where $r_{XY}$ is the Pearson correlation between dimensions $X$ and $Y$, $r_{XZ}$ and $r_{YZ}$ are their respective correlations with PC1 ($Z$), and $r_{XY \cdot Z}$ is the partial correlation between $X$ and $Y$ after removing the variance explained by PC1.
We assess reliability via ICC(1,1) and coefficient of variation, classify dimensions with inter-model $\sd < 0.15$ as alignment artifacts, and compare LLM inter-dimension correlations against published human baselines \citep{john1999bigfive} for 5 key dimension pairs.

\subsection{Cross-Model Discrimination}
\label{sec:discrimination}

All 9 dimensions show significant model effects ($p < 0.001$, FDR-corrected; Table~\ref{tab:anova_results}), with the strongest on HEXACO-H ($F = 56.75$, $\eta^2_p = 0.83$) and Neuroticism ($F = 34.50$, $\eta^2_p = 0.75$).
Kruskal-Wallis tests confirm these results.
Figure~\ref{fig:intervendor_sd} shows the inter-model $\sd$ by dimension: HEXACO-H has the highest (0.68), followed by Neuroticism (0.38) and Intuition (0.35); only Agreeableness ($\sd = 0.14$) falls below our alignment-artifact threshold.

Architecture type (Dense vs.\ MoE) shows suggestive but non-significant differences (Table~\ref{tab:architecture_effects}), with only 4 Dense families rendering all comparisons severely underpowered.
The strongest signal is on HEXACO-H ($d = 1.44$, 95\% CI $[0.14, 2.74]$, $p = 0.11$).
These patterns are explored further in \S\ref{sec:hexaco_h}.

\subsection{Inter-Dimension Structure}
\label{sec:interdim}

The 9$\times$9 model-level correlation matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:inter_dim_corr}) reveals that the BFI five-factor structure does not recover in our sample of LLMs.
The Conscientiousness--Neuroticism correlation is strongly positive ($r = +0.77$, 95\% CI $[0.42, 0.92]$, $p = 0.0008$), the reverse of the human baseline ($r = -0.25$).
The reversal is positive across all tested subgroups: MoE ($r = +0.66$, $p = 0.05$), small models ($r = +0.83$, $p = 0.04$), and open-source ($r = +0.70$, $p = 0.007$); it is non-significant for Dense models ($p = 0.44$, $n = 4$).

\paragraph{Why does the C--N correlation reverse?}
We offer an alignment-based explanation.
In humans, Conscientiousness (self-discipline, organization) and Neuroticism (emotional instability) are weakly negatively correlated because they tap independent biological and environmental factors \citep{john1999bigfive}.
In LLMs, both dimensions appear to be compressed toward the scale floor by safety alignment: models learn to present themselves as both emotionally stable (low Neuroticism) and diligent (high Conscientiousness) because both are socially desirable.
This shared alignment pressure induces a positive correlation that does not exist in humans, where individual differences in these traits arise from genuinely independent sources.
The HEXACO-H results (\S\ref{sec:hexaco_h}) are consistent with this account: models with more intensive alignment (larger MoE models) show stronger floor compression on morally sensitive items.
If this interpretation is correct, the C--N reversal is not an artifact of our method but a signature of systematic alignment-induced distortion of the psychometric response space.

Additional divergences include Extraversion flipping from positively to negatively correlated with Openness ($r = -0.62$, $p = 0.014$), and Agreeableness showing a stronger positive correlation with Conscientiousness ($r = +0.56$) than in humans ($r = +0.20$).
An exploratory PCA yields a three-factor solution (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_pca}), but this is severely underpowered ($n = 15$).

\subsection{HEXACO-H: Architecture and Scale}
\label{sec:hexaco_h}

Having identified HEXACO-H as the most discriminative dimension, we examine what drives its variation.
HEXACO-H items involve moral judgment (\eg ``I would buy stolen goods if I were sure I wouldn't get caught'') and are directly targeted by safety alignment during training.
Figure~\ref{fig:hexaco_h_vendors} shows a bimodal pattern: Dense models (Hunyuan: 2.77, KAT: 2.43, InternLM: 2.12, Seed: 1.15) tend to score higher than MoE models (ERNIE: 1.00, Qwen: 1.02, MiniMax: 1.08, Kimi: 1.10), suggesting that MoE models undergo more intensive moral safety alignment.

Dense models average $H = 2.12$ vs.\ MoE models $H = 1.36$ ($d = 1.44$, 95\% CI $[0.14, 2.74]$, $p = 0.11$; Table~\ref{tab:architecture_effects}), but the wide confidence interval and small Dense sample ($n = 4$) make this fragile: excluding Hunyuan reduces $d$ to 1.09 ($p = 0.29$).
The Dense and MoE groups are not balanced across providers, so architecture and provider effects cannot be disentangled.
In the broader 27-model sample, H correlates negatively with log-transformed parameters ($r = -0.49$, $p = 0.009$; Figure~\ref{fig:hexaco_h_params}), indicating that larger models score lower---consistent with more intensive safety alignment.
Additional scale effects are reported in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_params}.
H's high $\sd$ may be partially amplified by floor effects (5/15 models score at or below 1.15), but the Dense--MoE ranking is stable across all tested thresholds.

\subsection{Convergent Validity: No Evidence}
\label{sec:validity}

We compare LLM inter-dimension correlations against human baselines \citep{john1999bigfive} for 5 key dimension pairs (Table~\ref{tab:convergent_validity}; full matrix in Figure~\ref{fig:inter_dim_corr}).
Three of five pairs agree in sign, including Extraversion--Neuroticism (human $r = -0.30$, LLM $r = -0.65$).
Two pairs disagree: Extraversion--Agreeableness flips from positive to negative, and Conscientiousness--Neuroticism reverses to $r = +0.77$.
The 60\% sign agreement rate is not significantly above chance (binomial $p = 0.50$), constituting a null result: we find no evidence that the measured dimensions capture constructs equivalent to human personality.
The systematic nature of the divergences---the C--N reversal is one of the strongest correlations in our data---suggests a reorganization of trait structure rather than random noise, consistent with alignment-induced compression of the response space toward socially desirable endpoints.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\FloatBarrier

We presented a large-scale cross-model psychometric comparison across 15 model families, 9 dimensions, 61 items, and 12 seeds per model, all administered via unified APIs.
Our evidence points to a clear conclusion: \textit{LLMs do not share the personality structure of humans when probed with standard psychometric instruments.}
Three findings support this.
First, the inter-dimension correlation structure diverges sharply: the Conscientiousness--Neuroticism correlation reverses from $r = -0.25$ in humans to $r = +0.77$ in LLMs (95\% CI $[0.42, 0.92]$), consistent with alignment-driven compression of both dimensions toward socially desirable endpoints.
Second, convergent validity against human baselines is not supported (3/5 sign agreement, $p = 0.50$), meaning BFI dimension labels cannot be assumed to measure the same constructs in LLMs as in humans.
Third, model families differ substantially in their response distributions (median pairwise $d = 1.08$), with HEXACO-H showing the largest between-family effect ($\eta^2_p = 0.83$) and strong evidence that alignment intensity---not model architecture alone---drives this variation.

These findings challenge the growing practice of interpreting LLM psychometric scores as personality traits.
We propose an alignment-based explanation: safety training compresses the response space toward socially desirable endpoints, collapsing the natural variance that gives rise to the human personality factor structure.
Following \citet{binz2023psychology}, we treat psychometric items as standardized stimuli that probe response tendencies, and our results reinforce this stance: the label ``Extraversion'' or ``Neuroticism'' applied to an LLM does not denote the same construct it denotes in humans.
We encourage the community to treat LLM psychometric responses as measures of response style---systematic tendencies shaped by training data, alignment objectives, and deployment configurations---rather than as evidence for human-like personality.
Future work should test this alignment-compression hypothesis directly by comparing aligned and base models on the same instruments.

% ============================================================
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations constrain interpretation.
First, our sample of model families is small ($n = 15$); the structural claims (correlation reversal, validity failure) are based on aggregate statistics and should be replicated with a broader and more diverse set of models.
The exploratory PCA is severely underpowered at this sample size.
Second, only 2 of 15 models are closed-source (GPT-5, Claude Opus~4.5); findings may not generalize to the broader population of closed-source models, and the open-source--closed-source comparison (13 vs.\ 2) is underpowered.
Third, architecture, training intensity, and provider are partially confounded: we cannot attribute observed differences to any single factor, and the Dense--MoE comparison (4 vs.\ 9 families) does not reach significance.
Fourth, psychometric scales were designed for human self-report; the convergent validity null result (3/5 sign agreement) means the BFI dimension labels should not be interpreted as measuring the same constructs in LLMs as in humans.
Fifth, our alignment-compression hypothesis is inferred from aggregate patterns rather than tested directly; a definitive test would require comparing aligned and base models from the same family on the same instruments.
Sixth, this is a descriptive study---we quantify patterns without establishing causal mechanisms, though we provide an alignment-based account that can be tested in future work.
Seventh, within-family trajectory analyses have 2--6 data points per family and are descriptive.
Finally, our use of temperature $= 0.7$ introduces response variance that may not generalize to low-temperature deployments.

\section{Exploratory PCA Details}
\label{sec:appendix_pca}

We present the full exploratory PCA results here, with the caveat that PCA on $n = 15$ model families with 9 dimensions is severely underpowered and should be treated as hypothesis-generating.

\paragraph{Decomposition.}
We decompose the 9-dimension model-level correlation matrix ($n = 31$ models including within-family variants) via PCA:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{W}\mathbf{\Lambda}^{1/2}\mathbf{V}^\top
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{\Lambda} = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_9)$ and $\sum_{k} \lambda_k / \sum_j \lambda_j$ gives the proportion of variance explained.

\paragraph{Factor structure.}
PC1 (48.4\% variance) loads most strongly on Conscientiousness, Neuroticism, and Intuition (all negative), with Collectivism and Uncertainty Avoidance loading positively.
PC2 (17.4\% variance) isolates Collectivism ($+0.62$) and Agreeableness ($+0.61$).
PC3 (10.0\% variance) loads most strongly on Uncertainty Avoidance ($-0.71$).
These three factors explain 75.8\% of variance, compared to the five BFI factors expected in humans.
The factor structure does not cleanly recover the expected Big Five organization, consistent with the inter-dimension correlation divergences reported in \S\ref{sec:interdim}.

\paragraph{Extraversion reversal.}
Extraversion is significantly negatively correlated with Neuroticism ($r = -0.65$, $p = 0.009$) and Openness ($r = -0.62$, $p = 0.014$), with moderate negative correlations with Conscientiousness ($r = -0.40$, $p = 0.14$) and HEXACO-H ($r = -0.50$, $p = 0.06$) that do not reach significance.
In humans, Extraversion is positively correlated with Openness; in LLMs the sign flips.
Models that score high on Extraversion tend to be emotionally stable but closed-minded---a pattern with no direct human personality analog.

\paragraph{Agreeableness.}
Agreeableness shows a significant positive correlation with Conscientiousness ($r = +0.56$, $p = 0.030$) and a borderline correlation with Openness ($r = +0.49$, $p = 0.066$), but has no significant correlation with Collectivism ($r = -0.02$).
In humans, Agreeableness correlates moderately with Conscientiousness ($r = +0.20$) and weakly with Openness ($r = +0.10$); in LLMs, the A--C connection is substantially stronger.

\paragraph{Interpretation caution.}
Whether the observed factor structure generalizes to larger and more diverse model samples remains an open question.
As a rule of thumb, factor analysis typically requires at least 5--10 observations per variable; with $n = 15$ families and 9 dimensions, we fall below even the most lenient recommendation.
These results should be treated as suggestive patterns that motivate future investigation, not as evidence for a specific factor structure.
