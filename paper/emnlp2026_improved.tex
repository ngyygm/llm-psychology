\begin{abstract}
Human personality is shaped by trade-offs: people who are self-disciplined tend to be calmer, and outgoing people tend to be more curious. In large language models, these trade-offs vanish. We test 33 models from 15 families on 61 questionnaire items (12 random seeds each) and find three results. (1)~Different model families give noticeably different answers (median pairwise $d = 1.01$), but all scores cluster near the middle of the scale. (2)~The link between Conscientiousness and Neuroticism, which is reliably negative in humans ($r = -0.30$), \textbf{flips to strongly positive} in LLMs ($r = +0.76$, 95\% CI $[0.58, 0.88]$), and this holds across every subgroup we test. (3)~Small wording changes in prompts shift scores enough to show these measurements are not stable.
These results suggest that LLM questionnaire scores reflect response habits shaped by training, not genuine personality. We recommend treating such scores as behavioral probes rather than personality measurements.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Human personality is shaped by trade-offs.
Self-disciplined people tend to be emotionally stable; outgoing people are usually more open to new experiences \citep{mccrae1992five, soto2017bfi2}.
These patterns are not coincidences; they reflect real tensions between how people regulate emotions and pursue goals.
A growing number of studies give personality questionnaires to LLMs and treat the resulting scores as evidence of model ``personality'' \citep{jiang2023personality, pan2024personality, serapio-garcia2025personality}.
But a key question remains largely unasked: \emph{do LLM scores reproduce the trade-offs that define human personality?}
If not---if being conscientious and being neurotic no longer pull in opposite directions---then labeling LLMs with personality terms is misleading at best.

We argue that LLM questionnaire answers are better understood as \textit{response habits}: systematic patterns driven by training data and alignment, not genuine personality \citep{paulhus1991response, binz2023psychology}.
If so, two things should follow: (1)~different models should give noticeably different answers, and (2)~the relationships between personality dimensions should look different from the human pattern.

Prior work offers partial evidence.
\citet{schaekermann2024personality} found large differences across 9 LLMs, and \citet{serapio-garcia2025personality} tested 18 models but used different APIs and did not check structural validity.
\citet{salecha2024social} and \citet{dominguez-olmedo2024questioning} showed that LLM answers are compressed toward middle values, and \citet{braun2025acquiescence} found that models tend to agree with whatever is stated.
However, no study has tested whether the \emph{relationships between dimensions} in LLMs match the human pattern---a necessary check before claiming the scores mean the same thing.

We fill this gap with the largest cross-family comparison to date: 33 models from 15 families, 9 personality dimensions, 61 items, 12 seeds per model, all tested through the same APIs (Figure~\ref{fig:concept}).
We make three contributions.
\textbf{First,} we show that training leaves measurable fingerprints: 15 model families under the same test conditions produce large behavioral differences (median pairwise Cohen's $d = 1.01$).
\textbf{Second,} we reveal a missing trade-off: the link between Conscientiousness and Neuroticism, reliably negative in humans ($r = -0.30$) \citep{soto2017bfi2}, \textbf{flips to strongly positive} in LLMs ($r = +0.76$, 95\% CI $[0.58, 0.88]$, $p < 0.001$, $n = 33$).
This flip holds in every subgroup we test and is the clearest evidence that human personality structure does not carry over to LLMs.
\textbf{Third,} we show that prompt wording alone shifts scores (prompt explains 1--9\% of variance vs.\ 10--78\% for model family), with some models changing rank order across framings, confirming these measurements are not stable.

Together, these findings challenge reading LLM scores as personality: model families do have distinct behavioral fingerprints, but the structural backbone of human personality---its characteristic trade-offs---is absent.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{LLM personality measurement.}
Many studies give personality questionnaires to LLMs and read the results as trait profiles.
\citet{jiang2023personality} found that ChatGPT's scores change with temperature settings; \citet{pan2024personality} compared models from different cultural backgrounds; \citet{schaekermann2024personality} tested 9 LLMs and found large differences between them.
\citet{serapio-garcia2025personality} proposed a framework for measuring personality across 18 LLMs---the largest study before ours---but used different APIs for different models and did not test whether the score structure matches humans.
Other work shows that model size affects scores \citep{hilliard2024eliciting}, that scores drift over time \citep{bodroza2024personality}, that LLMs deviate from human norms \citep{sorokovikova2024big}, and that scores are unstable across scales and conversation contexts \citep{tosato2025instability}.
These studies show that LLMs produce variable, model-dependent scores, but none checks whether the \emph{relationships between dimensions} match the human pattern---a necessary condition for claiming the scores measure the same thing.

\paragraph{Trade-offs in human personality.}
The Five-Factor Model \citep{mccrae1992five} is the theoretical foundation for our work.
Large validation studies consistently find that personality dimensions pull against each other: Conscientiousness and Neuroticism ($r = -0.30$), Extraversion and Neuroticism ($r = -0.34$), Openness and Agreeableness ($r = +0.14$) \citep{soto2017bfi2}.
These trade-offs are not measurement artifacts---they reflect real psychological tensions. Self-disciplined people tend to be calmer because self-regulation and emotional volatility work against each other.
If LLM scores represent the same constructs, these trade-offs should appear in the data.
\citet{suh2024rediscovering} recovered Big Five structure from LLM word embeddings, but word similarity and questionnaire answers are different things; we test the latter.
Our contribution is to directly check whether human trade-offs survive when LLMs answer personality questions.

\paragraph{Measurement validity concerns.}
Using human personality tests on LLMs raises basic validity questions \citep{schaekermann2024personality, serpell2024validity}.
\citet{suh2023challenging} question whether these tests are meaningful for LLMs at all, since LLMs cannot genuinely report on their own experience.
Other work shows that LLMs display social desirability bias \citep{salecha2024social}, give answers that cluster near the scale midpoint \citep{dominguez-olmedo2024questioning}, tend to agree with whatever is stated \citep{braun2025acquiescence}, and show a gap between what they say about themselves and how they actually behave \citep{han2025personality}.
\citet{xie2025aipsychobench} found systematic differences between LLM and human answer patterns.
While prior work has flagged individual biases, we are the first to document a \emph{structural flip}---the disappearance of the Conscientiousness--Neuroticism trade-off---across a large sample, and to show that prompt wording alone can shift scores.

\subsection{Psychometric Instruments}
\label{sec:instruments}

We use 9 dimensions measured by 61 questions on a 1--5 agreement scale (Table~\ref{tab:instruments}).
Five dimensions come from the Big Five Inventory \citep{john1999bigfive, mccrae1992five, soto2017bfi2}: Extraversion (8 items), Agreeableness (9), Conscientiousness (9), Neuroticism (8), and Openness (10).
We add four more: HEXACO Honesty-Humility (5 items; \citealp{lee2004hexaco}), Collectivism (4 items; \citealp{schwartz1992universals}), Intuition (4 items; cognitive style), and Uncertainty Avoidance (4 items; cultural dimensions).
For reverse-scored items, we flip the scale ($\text{score} = 6 - \text{original}$); each dimension score is the average of its items.

\subsection{What We Measure: Response Style}
\label{sec:response_style}

We call what we measure \textbf{response habits} rather than personality \citep{paulhus1991response, rammstedt2000response}.
In psychology, response habits (or ``response styles'') are systematic tendencies in how someone answers questionnaires, regardless of what the questions ask.
Common examples include always agreeing, always picking the middle option, always picking extreme values, and giving socially desirable answers \citep{baumgartner2007modeling}.
These habits belong to the \emph{answerer}, not to the trait being measured.

Crucially, personality is not just about individual scores---it is about the \textbf{trade-offs between dimensions}.
The fact that self-disciplined people tend to be less anxious ($r = -0.30$) \citep{soto2017bfi2} reflects a real psychological tension, not a measurement quirk.
If LLM scores do not show these trade-offs, then labels like ``Conscientiousness'' and ``Neuroticism'' do not mean the same thing as they do for humans.

We use the response-habit framing because LLMs lack the inner experience that personality concepts assume.
Our question is: \emph{do LLM answers reproduce the trade-offs that define human personality, or do they reflect habits shaped by training?}
We test this directly by comparing how dimensions relate to each other in LLMs versus published human data (Section~\ref{sec:interdim}).

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

Each model answers all 61 items per seed at temperature $= 0.7$, with 12 different seeds (0, 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 42, 123, 456) to capture response variation, totaling 10,980 API calls.
We choose temperature 0.7 to balance diversity with reproducibility: temperature 0 gives identical answers every time (eliminating within-model variation), while higher values ($\geq 1.0$) produce erratic responses.
\citet{jiang2023personality} showed that temperature affects personality scores, making a moderate setting necessary.
Items are phrased as self-descriptions (\eg ``I see myself as someone who is the life of the party'') with a 1--5 agreement scale.
We handle API rate limits with exponential backoff and up to 5 parallel threads.

\subsection{Analysis Framework}
\label{sec:analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} shows the full pipeline.
Our analysis has several parts.
For each dimension, we test whether models differ using one-way ANOVA (15 model families) with false-discovery-rate (FDR) correction \citep{benjamini1995controlling} and effect size ($\eta^2_p$), plus ordered logistic regression to handle the ordinal nature of Likert data.
We compute pairwise effect sizes (Cohen's $d$) for all 105 model pairs across 8 dimensions (840 tests, FDR-corrected); power analysis confirms we can detect large effects ($d \geq 0.8$) with $>80\%$ power given 12 observations per model.
To control for agreement bias, we compute partial correlations after removing the first principal component (PC1).
We check reliability using ICC(1,1) and coefficient of variation, flag dimensions with very low between-model spread ($\sd < 0.15$) as likely alignment artifacts, and compare how dimensions relate to each other in LLMs versus published human data \citep{john1999bigfive}.
For all analyses, we first average each model's 12-seed responses into dimension means, then compute statistics across model families ($n = 15$ for primary analyses; $n = 33$ for replication).
Human baseline correlations come from the BFI-2 validation study \citep{soto2017bfi2} ($N = 1{,}000$).

\subsection{Cross-Model Discrimination}
\label{sec:discrimination}

All 8 primary dimensions show significant model effects ($p < 0.001$, FDR-corrected; Table~\ref{tab:anova_results}), with the strongest differences on Neuroticism ($F = 34.60$, $\eta^2_p = 0.75$) and Intuition ($F = 20.26$, $\eta^2_p = 0.63$); non-parametric tests confirm these results.
Figure~\ref{fig:intermodel_sd} shows between-model variability by dimension: Intuition (0.37) and Neuroticism (0.34) vary most, while Agreeableness ($\sd = 0.15$) and UA ($\sd = 0.16$) are near our alignment-artifact threshold.
HEXACO-H has the highest variability (0.67) but is excluded from primary analysis due to poor reliability ($\alpha = 0.27$); details in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_hexaco_detail}.

Dense vs.\ MoE architecture shows significant differences on some dimensions (Table~\ref{tab:architecture_effects}), with 10 Dense and 5 MoE families.
The clearest signals are on Agreeableness ($d = 0.78$, $p < 0.001$) and UA ($d = -0.83$, $p < 0.001$).
\textbf{However, architecture is fully confounded with provider} (all MoE models come from a subset of providers, while Dense models include GPT and Claude) and partially confounded with scale, so these patterns describe co-variation, not causal effects.

The pairwise differences are large: the median Cohen's $d$ across all 105 model pairs and 8 primary dimensions is 1.01, and every pair exceeds $d \geq 0.8$ on at least one dimension (Figure~\ref{fig:cohen_d_heatmap}).
Table~\ref{tab:top_cohen_d} lists the largest effects per dimension.
Neuroticism and Conscientiousness are the most separable dimensions (max $d = 5.29$ and $4.45$), while Agreeableness shows the weakest separation (median $d = 0.74$).
GPT and Claude produce the smallest distance of any pair ($0.50$), hinting at convergent training goals across leading model families.
These large between-model differences show that personality questions work as behavioral probes distinguishing model families, consistent with our response-habit interpretation (\S\ref{sec:response_style}).
But do these differences reflect the trade-offs that define human personality? We turn to that question next.

\subsection{The Missing Trade-Off: Structural Divergence}
\label{sec:interdim}

Do LLM answers reproduce the trade-offs that define human personality?
We test this by comparing how dimensions relate to each other in LLMs versus published human data for 5 key dimension pairs (Table~\ref{tab:convergent_validity} in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_structure}).

The most striking result is the Conscientiousness--Neuroticism link.
In humans, these two dimensions pull in opposite directions ($r = -0.30$): self-disciplined people tend to be calmer, reflecting a real tension between self-regulation and emotional reactivity \citep{soto2017bfi2}.
In LLMs, this trade-off \textbf{flips to strongly positive} ($r = +0.76$, 95\% CI $[0.58, 0.88]$, $p < 0.001$; Figure~\ref{fig:inter_dim_corr}).
The flip is robust: it replicates at $n = 33$ models with a narrow confidence interval that excludes the human baseline, holds across all subgroups (Dense, MoE, open-source, closed-source), and persists after correcting for agreement bias (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_structure}).
A convergent validity check yields 4/5 sign matches with human baselines (binomial $p = 0.38$), showing broad structural similarity; the single critical divergence---the C--N flip---is individually significant and is our main structural finding.

What does this flip mean?
In humans, high Conscientiousness and low Neuroticism are psychologically opposed: self-discipline trades off against emotional volatility.
In LLMs, both dimensions move together: models scoring higher on Conscientiousness also score higher on Neuroticism.
This pattern fits a shared mechanism: both dimensions are pushed toward the scale midpoint by the same training pressures, creating a positive link through shared response compression rather than through the psychological tension that produces the negative link in humans.
The C--N flip directly challenges applying standard personality labels to LLMs: if ``Conscientiousness'' and ``Neuroticism'' no longer relate to each other as they do in humans, then at least one label does not mean the same thing.

\subsection{Prompt Sensitivity}
\label{sec:prompt_sensitivity}

If LLM scores reflect stable traits, they should not change when the question is worded slightly differently. We gave the same 61 items to all 15 models under four prompt conditions (12 seeds each, temperature $= 0.7$): \textbf{Default} (``respond as honestly as possible''), \textbf{Neutral} (instruction removed, ``rate your agreement''), \textbf{Persona} (human role-play: ``you are completing a personality survey''), and \textbf{Direct} (no instruction, only the item and scale).
All 15 models have complete data across all four conditions.

\paragraph{Score shifts.}
The average within-model standard deviation across the four prompts was 0.133 on the 1--5 scale (range 0.095--0.205 across dimensions).
HEXACO-H and Neuroticism were most sensitive to prompt wording (mean SD $= 0.205$ and $0.189$), while Agreeableness and Uncertainty Avoidance were most stable (mean SD $= 0.095$ and $0.102$).
Cohen's $d$ between each variant and the Default was a median $|d|$ of 0.22; the largest effects were on Conscientiousness ($d = 0.70$ for Neutral) and Collectivism ($d = 0.51$ for Direct).
Overall, model family effects (Table~\ref{tab:prompt_variance}) are an order of magnitude larger than prompt effects.

\paragraph{Rank stability.}
Model rankings are mostly preserved across prompt wordings.
Average Spearman $\rho$ between rankings was 0.60; HEXACO-H had the most stable rankings (mean $\rho = 0.87$), while Agreeableness had the least ($\rho = 0.22$ for Direct vs.\ Default).
Most dimensions keep their rank order, though the Direct condition occasionally flips rankings (negative $\rho$ for Extraversion and Agreeableness).

\paragraph{Response habit shifts.}
Prompt wording also shifts how models use the scale (Table~\ref{tab:prompt_response_styles}).
The Neutral prompt increased extreme answering from 22.7\% (Default) to 31.3\%, while the Persona prompt reduced it to 17.6\%.
Midpoint preference ranged from 40.4\% (Direct) to 48.6\% (Neutral).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper asks a simple question: do LLM questionnaire answers reproduce the trade-offs that define human personality? Across 33 models and 61 items, the answer is no. In humans, personality dimensions pull against each other because self-regulation and emotional reactivity are genuinely opposed. In LLMs, these tensions vanish: answers cluster near the scale midpoint, and dimensions that should be inversely related become positively linked. The Conscientiousness--Neuroticism flip ($r = -0.30 \to +0.76$) is not a statistical fluke; it survives replication across subgroups and is the strongest evidence to date that human personality structure does not transfer to language models.

What does this mean in practice? We see three implications.

\textbf{First,} personality questionnaires should not be used as off-the-shelf tests for LLMs. The BFI, HEXACO, and similar tools were designed for human self-report, where a ``Conscientiousness'' score reflects years of real behavior. When an LLM scores high on Conscientiousness, it does not mean the model is organized or disciplined; it means training produced a particular pattern of scale responses. Reading these scores as personality risks false conclusions about model capabilities and safety.

\textbf{Second,} response-habit signatures can serve as a lightweight probe for detecting alignment changes. Our results show that model families differ substantially (median $d = 1.01$), and that aligned models compress their answers toward the midpoint compared to base models. This suggests that response patterns could supplement existing safety evaluations: a model that suddenly shifts from moderate to extreme answering on personality items may have undergone a meaningful alignment change. Developing this into a practical monitoring tool is a promising direction.

\textbf{Third,} the structural divergence we document matters for downstream applications. If personality scores are used as inputs to reward models, preference optimizers, or user modeling systems, practitioners should know that these scores do not carry the same meaning as in humans. A model scoring high on both Neuroticism and Conscientiousness is not ``contradictory''; it is simply showing the flattened answer structure that alignment produces.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Several directions follow. The most urgent is to develop questionnaires designed specifically for LLMs, rather than borrowing human tests. Such tools should target the dimensions that actually vary across models (response compression, agreement patterns, scale usage) and should be validated against behavioral outcomes rather than human norms. Second, it would be valuable to test whether the C--N flip generalizes beyond our sample to a more internationally diverse set of model families. Third, the mechanism behind the flattened structure remains unknown: experiments that vary alignment intensity while holding scale and architecture constant would help isolate alignment from other factors.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations constrain our conclusions.
First, our primary sample is 15 model families; correlations at this sample size have wide confidence intervals, which we address by replicating key findings with 33 models.
Second, personality scales were designed for human self-report; our human baselines come from one internet sample \citep{soto2017bfi2} and may not be universal, and the convergent validity result (4/5 sign match, $p = 0.38$) is inconclusive.
Third, our aligned-vs.-base comparison (Section~\ref{sec:aligned_base}) covers only 3 families, and the Qwen comparisons mix alignment with scale (8B/14B $\to$ 397B); same-scale pairs would give cleaner evidence.
Fourth, this is a descriptive study; we document patterns without establishing causes. Alignment is one plausible explanation among several, including training data composition, instruction tuning, scaling, and API-specific prompting.
Fifth, all tested models are aligned/instruct versions from commercial APIs; our thinking ablation (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_thinking}) adds evidence, but the base models represent only a subset of families.
Sixth, our prompt sensitivity experiment (Section~\ref{sec:prompt_sensitivity}) tests only four framings; additional framings might yield different results.
Seventh, our use of temperature $= 0.7$ adds response variance that may not generalize to low-temperature deployments.
Finally, finding a flattened structure does not mean LLMs lack all variation; they do vary across families, but this variation does not map onto human personality trade-offs.

\section{Exploratory PCA Details}
\label{sec:appendix_pca}

We present the full exploratory PCA results here, with the caveat that PCA on $n = 15$ model families with 9 dimensions is underpowered. We replicate the key structural findings (C--N reversal) with an expanded sample of 33 models.

\paragraph{Decomposition.}
We decompose the 9-dimension correlation matrix ($n = 31$ models including within-family variants) using PCA:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{W}\mathbf{\Lambda}^{1/2}\mathbf{V}^\top
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{\Lambda} = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_9)$ and the proportion of variance explained by each component is $\lambda_k / \sum_j \lambda_j$.

\paragraph{Factor structure.}
PC1 (51.1\% of variance) loads most strongly on HEXACO-H ($+0.82$), with secondary loadings on Intuition ($+0.31$) and Conscientiousness ($+0.29$).
PC2 (14.0\%) isolates Neuroticism ($+0.52$) and HEXACO-H ($-0.51$).
PC3 (11.0\%) is dominated by Collectivism ($-0.71$).
These three factors explain 76.1\% of variance, compared to the five factors expected from human data.
The structure does not cleanly recover the Big Five, consistent with the inter-dimension divergences reported in \S\ref{sec:interdim}.

\paragraph{Extraversion flip.}
Extraversion is significantly negatively correlated with Neuroticism ($r = -0.59$, $p < 0.001$) and Openness ($r = -0.51$, $p = 0.002$). The E--O sign flip replicates at $n = 33$ (95\% CI $[-0.69, -0.27]$).
In humans, Extraversion and Openness are positively linked; in LLMs the direction reverses.
Models high on Extraversion tend to be emotionally stable but less open---a pattern with no human analog.

\paragraph{Agreeableness.}
Agreeableness shows a positive correlation with Conscientiousness ($r = +0.27$, $p = 0.13$) that is comparable to the human baseline ($r = +0.28$; \citet{soto2017bfi2}).
Agreeableness also correlates weakly with Openness ($r = +0.12$, $p = 0.52$), similar to humans ($r = +0.14$).

\paragraph{Caution.}
Whether this factor structure generalizes to larger, more diverse model samples remains open.
Factor analysis typically needs at least 5--10 observations per variable; with $n = 15$ families and 9 dimensions, we fall short.
However, the expanded sample ($n = 33$) confirms the key finding---the C--N correlation flip---with high statistical significance ($p < 0.001$).
These results should be treated as suggestive patterns, not as evidence for a specific factor structure.

\section{Inter-Dimension Structure and Convergent Validity (Detailed)}
\label{sec:appendix_structure}

The main text (\S\ref{sec:interdim}) summarizes the key findings. Here we give the full details.

\subsection{Correlation Matrix}
The 9$\times$9 correlation matrix across model-level means (Figure~\ref{fig:inter_dim_corr}) shows that the Big Five factor structure does not recover in LLMs.
Most notably, Conscientiousness and Neuroticism are strongly positively correlated ($r = +0.76$, 95\% CI $[0.58, 0.88]$, $p < 0.001$), the reverse of the human baseline ($r = -0.30$; \citet{soto2017bfi2}).
This flip replicates robustly at $n = 33$ models ($r = +0.76$, 95\% CI $[0.58, 0.88]$, $p = 3.4 \times 10^{-7}$), and holds across subgroups: MoE ($r = +0.66$, $p = 0.05$), small models ($r = +0.83$, $p = 0.04$), and open-source ($r = +0.70$, $p = 0.007$), though it is non-significant for the small Dense subset ($p = 0.44$, $n = 4$).

\paragraph{Possible explanation: alignment-driven compression.}
In humans, Conscientiousness and Neuroticism are weakly independent \citep{john1999bigfive}.
In LLMs, both are pushed toward the scale midpoint by alignment, creating a positive link through shared compression rather than through genuine trait covariance.
This is consistent with the HEXACO-H results (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_hexaco_detail}), and the replication at $n = 33$ substantially reduces concern about small-sample artifacts.

Beyond the C--N flip, Extraversion is negatively linked to Openness ($r = -0.51$, 95\% CI $[-0.73, -0.15]$, $p = 0.003$), and Agreeableness shows a correlation with Conscientiousness ($r = +0.27$, 95\% CI $[-0.10, +0.65]$, $p = 0.134$) close to the human value ($r = +0.28$).

\subsection{Convergent Validity}
We compare how dimensions relate to each other in LLMs vs.\ published human data \citep{soto2017bfi2, vanderlinden2010general} for 5 key pairs (Table~\ref{tab:convergent_validity}), using the expanded sample of 33 models.
Four of five pairs agree in sign, including Extraversion--Neuroticism (human $r = -0.34$, LLM $r = -0.59$, 95\% CI $[-0.77, -0.25]$, $p < 0.001$) and Agreeableness--Conscientiousness (human $r = +0.28$, LLM $r = +0.27$, 95\% CI $[-0.10, +0.65]$).
The single disagreement is Conscientiousness--Neuroticism, which flips from $r = -0.30$ in humans to $r = +0.76$ in LLMs (95\% CI $[0.58, 0.88]$, $p < 0.001$).
The 80\% sign agreement is consistent with the null (binomial $p = 0.38$); it does not prove construct equivalence, but neither does it disprove it.
The one divergent pair is the most meaningful: in humans, C and N are weakly independent; in LLMs, both are compressed toward the midpoint, creating a positive link through shared response compression.
We note that the human baseline comes from one internet sample \citep{soto2017bfi2}; the $r = -0.30$ C--N value may not be universal.
