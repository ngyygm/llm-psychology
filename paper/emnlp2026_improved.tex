\begin{abstract}
A growing body of work applies personality inventories to LLMs, yet it remains unclear whether the resulting scores capture meaningful individual differences or systematic response tendencies shaped by training and alignment.
In this study, we administer 61 Likert-scale items spanning 9 psychometric dimensions to one model from each of 15 model families via unified APIs, collecting 10,980 responses (12 seeds per model), and characterize the resulting response style variation.
We find that model families exhibit large, systematic differences in their response distributions (median pairwise Cohen's $d = 1.01$), with Neuroticism showing the largest between-family effect ($\eta^2_p = 0.75$).
The inter-dimension correlation structure reorganizes relative to humans: the Conscientiousness--Neuroticism correlation reverses from $r = -0.25$ to $r = +0.76$ (95\% CI $[0.47, 0.92]$), and Extraversion--Openness flips from positive to negative.
Convergent validity against published human baselines yields insufficient evidence for construct equivalence (3/5 sign agreement, $p = 0.50$), further indicating that standard dimension labels do not transfer cleanly to LLMs.
Together, these findings demonstrate systematic response style variation across LLM families and quantify the degree to which psychometric responses are shaped by training and alignment rather than by human-like individual differences.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A growing number of studies apply personality inventories to LLMs, reporting that different models exhibit different trait profiles \citep{jiang2023personality, pan2024personality, serapio-garcia2025personality}.
These results are often interpreted as evidence that LLMs possess personality-like traits shaped by training and alignment.
However, if LLMs respond to personality items using surface-level heuristics---such as mimicking socially desirable answers or following alignment-induced response tendencies---then the resulting scores may capture systematic \textit{response styles} rather than personality traits, and the inter-dimension correlations may differ fundamentally from the human factor structure.
Following \citet{binz2023psychology}, we use the term \textit{response style} to describe observed response patterns without claiming they reflect personality traits or inner states.

We characterize this response style variation with a large-scale cross-model comparison (Figure~\ref{fig:concept}).
Using unified API platforms, we administer 61 Likert-scale items spanning 9 dimensions---the Big Five Inventory \citep{john1999bigfive}, HEXACO Honesty-Humility \citep{lee2004hexaco}, Schwartz Values \citep{schwartz1992universals}, cognitive style, and cultural dimensions---to one model from each of 15 model families, repeating each query across 12 random seeds (10,980 total API calls).
We also track 16 additional models from three families (Qwen, DeepSeek, GLM) across versions, yielding 31 models total.

We present three findings that characterize systematic response style variation across LLM families:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Model families exhibit distinct response style signatures.}
    Families differ substantially in their response distributions (median pairwise Cohen's $d = 1.01$), indicating that different training approaches produce systematic behavioral fingerprints.
    \item \textbf{Inter-dimension correlations reorganize relative to humans.}
    The Conscientiousness--Neuroticism correlation reverses from $r = -0.25$ in humans to $r = +0.76$ in LLMs (95\% CI $[0.47, 0.92]$), and Extraversion--Openness flips from positive to negative, patterns observed across most tested subgroups.
    \item \textbf{Standard dimension labels do not transfer cleanly.}
    Comparison against published human baselines yields only 3/5 sign agreement ($p = 0.50$); this inconclusive convergent validity result itself constitutes evidence for trait structure reorganization rather than random noise.
\end{enumerate}

We test convergent validity against human baselines (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_structure}), correct for acquiescence bias (\S\ref{sec:acquiescence}), and report 95\% confidence intervals for key statistics.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{LLM personality and response behavior.}
\citet{jiang2023personality} found temperature-dependent personality variation in ChatGPT using the BFI-10.
\citet{pan2024personality} compared LLMs from different cultural backgrounds on the BFI-44, reporting cultural effects.
\citet{myska2023personality} showed that GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 produce stable profiles across sessions, and \citet{schaekermann2024personality} examined 9 LLMs on the Big Five, finding moderate-to-large inter-model differences.
\citet{serapio-garcia2025personality} proposed a psychometric framework for evaluating and shaping personality traits across 18 LLMs, reporting that larger models exhibit broader trait ranges.
\citet{hilliard2024eliciting} confirmed that model scale affects elicited personality profiles, with larger models showing more extreme trait scores.
\citet{bodroza2024personality} tested 7 LLMs on multiple personality instruments, finding limited temporal stability but consistently elevated prosociality.
\citet{santos2024personality} surveyed personality assessment with LLMs, highlighting both promises and pitfalls of treating model outputs as psychometric data.
\citet{sorokovikova2024big} replicated Big Five profiles across three models and found systematic deviations from human norms, while \citet{tosato2025instability} documented persistent instability across scales and conversation histories.
While \citet{serapio-garcia2025personality} evaluated the largest set of models to date (18), their study used heterogeneous APIs and did not test for structural differences in inter-dimension correlations.
Our work contributes unified API control across 15 model families and explicit validity testing against human baselines.

\paragraph{Behavioral evaluation beyond capabilities.}
Standard capability benchmarks \citep{hendrycks2021measuring, liang2022helm} measure task performance---reasoning, coding, instruction-following---and instruction-following evaluators such as AlpacaEval \citep{dubois2024alpacaeval} assess response quality, but none address how models respond to self-referential statements about preferences and moral tendencies.
\citet{laverghetta2022predicting} showed that language models can predict human psychometric properties from text, raising the question of whether LLM self-reports reflect similar constructs.
We contribute a behavioral evaluation dimension: how models respond to standardized self-referential questions.

\paragraph{Response biases and measurement validity.}
Applying human-designed psychometric instruments to LLMs raises validity concerns \citep{schaekermann2024personality, serpell2024validity}.
\citet{suh2023challenging} question whether standard personality tests are valid for LLMs at all, given the lack of genuine self-report.
\citet{salecha2024social} demonstrated that LLMs exhibit human-like social desirability biases in Big Five survey responses, inflating socially desirable traits.
\citet{dominguez-olmedo2024questioning} showed that LLM survey responses lack the natural variation seen in human populations, producing compressed response distributions.
\citet{tjuatja2023response} found that LLMs fail to replicate known human response biases in survey design.
\citet{braun2025acquiescence} documented acquiescence bias in LLMs, systematically agreeing with questionnaire statements regardless of content.
\citet{han2025personality} demonstrated a dissociation between LLM self-reported personality and behavioral choices, reinforcing the need to distinguish stated preferences from actions.
\citet{xie2025aipsychobench} introduced AIPsychoBench, a benchmark specifically designed to measure psychometric differences between LLMs and humans, finding systematic discrepancies in response patterns.
We address these concerns by framing measurement explicitly as ``response style'' rather than personality, testing convergent validity against human data, and correcting for acquiescence bias---extending methodological transparency beyond prior work.

\paragraph{Factor structure in LLMs.}
The Five-Factor Model \citep{mccrae1992five} provides the theoretical foundation for the BFI dimensions we use.
\citet{suh2024rediscovering} recovered the Big Five factor structure from LLMs using singular value decomposition on log-probabilities of personality-relevant adjectives.
Their approach analyzes semantic space, whereas we analyze questionnaire responses---two complementary probes.
An exploratory PCA on our data hints at a three-factor reorganization (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_pca}), but this is severely underpowered ($n = 15$).

\label{sec:instruments}

\subsection{What We Measure: Response Style}
\label{sec:response_style}

We frame our measurements as \textbf{response style} rather than personality \citep{paulhus1991response, rammstedt2000response}.
In psychometrics, response style refers to systematic tendencies in how respondents answer questionnaire items, independent of item content: acquiescence (yea-saying), midpoint responding, extreme responding, and socially desirable responding \citep{baumgartner2007modeling}.
These styles are properties of the \emph{respondent}, not of the construct being measured---a respondent who agrees with every statement is not necessarily more extraverted or agreeable.

We adopt this framing because LLMs lack the subjective experience that personality constructs presume.
When an LLM reports high Conscientiousness, this reflects a systematic tendency in its training data and alignment to produce conscientiousness-consistent responses, not a genuine psychological trait.
Our research question is therefore descriptive: \emph{do models from different families exhibit systematically different response style signatures when presented with identical psychometric items?}
We report dimension labels (``Extraversion,'' ``Neuroticism'') for interpretability but explicitly test (and fail to establish) that these labels capture constructs equivalent to human personality (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_structure}).

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

Each model is administered all 61 items per seed at temperature $= 0.7$, with 12 seeds (0, 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 42, 123, 456) capturing response distribution variation, totaling 10,980 API calls.
We set temperature to 0.7 to balance response diversity with reproducibility: temperature 0 yields deterministic outputs that eliminate within-model variance, while higher temperatures ($\geq 1.0$) may produce inconsistent self-referential responses.
Prior work on LLM personality \citep{jiang2023personality} has shown that temperature affects reported trait scores, making a moderate temperature necessary to capture the full range of each model's response tendencies.
Items are framed as self-referential statements (\eg ``I see myself as someone who is the life of the party'') with a 1--5 agreement scale.
Rate limiting uses exponential backoff on 429 responses with up to 5 concurrent threads.

\subsection{Analysis Framework}
\label{sec:analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} illustrates the full experimental pipeline.
Our analysis combines several complementary approaches.
For each dimension, we run a one-way ANOVA (Model Family, 15 levels) with FDR-corrected $p$-values \citep{benjamini1995controlling} and partial $\eta^2_p$, supplemented by ordered logistic regression to account for the ordinal nature of Likert responses.
The effect size is quantified as partial $\eta^2_p$:
\begin{equation}
\eta^2_p = \frac{SS_{\text{between}}}{SS_{\text{between}} + SS_{\text{within}}}
\end{equation}
where $SS_{\text{between}}$ and $SS_{\text{within}}$ are the between-group and within-group sums of squares, respectively.
Pairwise Cohen's $d$ (Welch's $t$-test) is computed for all 105 model pairs across 8 primary dimensions (840 tests, FDR-corrected):
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
d_{ij} &= \frac{\bar{x}_i - \bar{x}_j}{s_{\text{pooled}}}, \\[4pt]
s_{\text{pooled}} &= \sqrt{\frac{(n_i{-}1)s_i^2 + (n_j{-}1)s_j^2}{n_i + n_j - 2}}
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{x}_i$ and $\bar{x}_j$ are the mean dimension scores for model families $i$ and $j$, $s_i$ and $s_j$ are their standard deviations, $n_i$ and $n_j$ are the number of observations (12 seeds each), and $s_{\text{pooled}}$ is the pooled standard deviation.
Power analysis confirms adequate power ($>80\%$) for $d \geq 0.8$ with 12 observations per model.
To correct for acquiescence bias, we compute partial correlations controlling for the first principal component (PC1):
\begin{equation}
r_{XY \cdot Z} = \frac{r_{XY} - r_{XZ} r_{YZ}}{\sqrt{(1-r_{XZ}^2)(1-r_{YZ}^2)}}
\end{equation}
where $r_{XY}$ is the Pearson correlation between dimensions $X$ and $Y$, $r_{XZ}$ and $r_{YZ}$ are their respective correlations with PC1 ($Z$), and $r_{XY \cdot Z}$ is the partial correlation between $X$ and $Y$ after removing the variance explained by PC1.
We assess reliability via ICC(1,1) and coefficient of variation, classify dimensions with inter-model $\sd < 0.15$ as alignment artifacts, and compare LLM inter-dimension correlations against published human baselines \citep{john1999bigfive} for 5 key dimension pairs.
For all correlation and ANOVA analyses, we first aggregate the 12 seed-level responses per model into dimension means, then compute statistics at the model-family level ($n = 15$).
The human baseline correlations are drawn from the large-scale validation of the BFI-44 reported in the BFI-2 manual \citep{oconnor2007bfi} and the trait taxonomy chapter \citep{john1999bigfive}; exact standard deviations for these inter-dimension correlations are not always available in the original sources.

\subsection{Cross-Model Discrimination}
\label{sec:discrimination}

All 8 primary dimensions show significant model effects ($p < 0.001$, FDR-corrected; Table~\ref{tab:anova_results}), with the strongest discrimination on Neuroticism ($F = 34.60$, $\eta^2_p = 0.75$) and Intuition ($F = 20.26$, $\eta^2_p = 0.63$); non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis tests confirm these results.
Figure~\ref{fig:intermodel_sd} shows the inter-model $\sd$ by dimension: among the 8 primary dimensions, Intuition (0.37) and Neuroticism (0.34) show the most variability, while Agreeableness ($\sd = 0.15$) and UA ($\sd = 0.16$) fall below our alignment-artifact threshold of 0.15.
HEXACO-H has the highest overall variability (0.67) but is excluded from primary analysis ($\alpha = 0.27$); detailed results are in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_hexaco_detail}.

Architecture type (Dense vs.\ MoE) shows significant differences on several dimensions (Table~\ref{tab:architecture_effects}), with 10 Dense and 5 MoE families.
The strongest signals are on Agreeableness ($d = 0.78$, $p < 0.001$) and UA ($d = -0.83$, $p < 0.001$); HEXACO-H shows an even larger apparent effect ($d = 0.91$) but is excluded from primary analysis due to low reliability.
\textbf{However, architecture is fully confounded with provider} (all MoE models are from Chinese providers, while Dense models include GPT and Claude) and partially confounded with model scale, so these patterns must be interpreted as descriptive observations of co-variation rather than evidence for causal architecture effects.

\subsection{Inter-Dimension Correlations (Exploratory)}
\label{sec:interdim}

The 9$\times$9 model-level correlation matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:inter_dim_corr}) suggests that the human BFI factor structure does not fully recover in LLMs.
The most notable divergence is the Conscientiousness--Neuroticism correlation, which is strongly positive in LLMs ($r = +0.76$, 95\% CI $[0.47, 0.92]$) but weakly negative in humans ($r = -0.25$), and this reversal holds across multiple subgroups (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_structure}).
A convergent validity test against human baselines yields 3/5 sign agreement (binomial $p = 0.50$), providing no evidence that the measured dimensions capture constructs equivalent to human personality (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_structure}).

These structural observations are \textbf{exploratory} ($n = 15$ families) and should be interpreted with caution; the wide confidence intervals at this sample size mean that replication with a broader model sample is needed before drawing firm conclusions about factor structure reorganization.

\label{sec:aligned_base}

Despite these caveats, if response style variation reflects inherent model properties rather than alignment, base and aligned models should show similar profiles.

\subsection{Within-Family Evolution}
\label{sec:evolution}

To examine whether response styles change across model versions, we track 16 additional models from three families (Qwen: 6 versions; DeepSeek: 4 versions; GLM: 6 versions; full trajectories in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_trajectories}).
Most dimensions show moderate variation without clear monotonic trends.
Notable exceptions: DeepSeek shows a steep decline on HEXACO-H from V2.5 ($H = 3.00$) to V3.2 ($H = 1.68$), consistent with intensified safety alignment between versions (though HEXACO-H is excluded from primary analysis); GLM shows the largest within-family shift on Intuition (5.0 to 3.875).
These observations are descriptive and motivate future controlled experiments.

\subsection{Acquiescence and Reliability}
\label{sec:acquiescence}

\paragraph{Response style indicators.}
Following \citet{paulhus1991response}, we operationalize three response style indicators directly from item-level responses (Figure~\ref{fig:response_styles}).
\textbf{Midpoint responding} (proportion of responses at the scale midpoint, 3.0) varies widely across families: Step (83\%) and KAT (87\%) show extreme midpoint bias, while MiniMax (20\%) and Claude (27\%) show low midpoint responding.
\textbf{Extreme responding} (proportion of responses at 1 or 5) shows the opposite pattern: Ring (53\%) and Seed (43\%) produce the most extreme responses, while Hunyuan (2\%) and Claude (4\%) rarely use scale endpoints.
\textbf{Acquiescence bias} (mean of positively worded items minus mean of reverse-scored items) is consistently negative across 14 of 15 families (mean $= -0.28$), indicating a slight tendency to disagree with positively worded items and agree with negatively worded items; Ring is the sole exception ($+0.24$).
These indicators confirm that models differ substantially in how they use the Likert scale, consistent with the response style framework.

\subsection{Prompt Sensitivity}
\label{sec:prompt_sensitivity}

If LLM psychometric scores reflect stable traits, they should be invariant to surface-level changes in question framing. We administered the same 61 Likert items to all 15 Study~1 models under four prompt conditions (12 seeds each, temperature $= 0.7$): \textbf{Default} (``respond as honestly as possible''), \textbf{Neutral} (instruction removed, ``rate your agreement''), \textbf{Persona} (human role-play: ``you are completing a personality survey''), and \textbf{Direct} (no instruction, only the item and scale).
Two models (GLM-5, Ring-flash-2.0) were unavailable for the Direct condition due to API downtime; 13 models have complete data across all four conditions.

\paragraph{Score shifts.}
Within-model standard deviation across the four prompt conditions averaged 0.127 on the 1--5 scale (range 0.083--0.174 across dimensions).
Conscientiousness and Neuroticism showed the largest prompt sensitivity (mean SD $= 0.173$ and $0.174$), while Agreeableness and Uncertainty Avoidance were most stable (mean SD $= 0.083$ and $0.093$).
Cohen's $d$ between each variant and the Default condition yielded a median $|d|$ of 0.49--0.98 across dimensions; 75\% of model-dimension pairs exceeded $|d| > 0.5$, comparable to the between-model effect sizes reported in Section~\ref{sec:discrimination}.

\paragraph{Rank instability.}
Model rankings are not preserved across prompt framings.
Spearman $\rho$ between model rankings under different prompts averaged 0.28--0.65 across dimensions (mean 0.49); for Agreeableness, the minimum pairwise $\rho$ was $-0.13$, indicating rank reversals.
Even for the most stable dimensions (Conscientiousness, Intuition; mean $\rho = 0.65$), the lowest pairwise correlations were only 0.35, far below the $\rho > 0.80$ threshold that would indicate stable relative ordering.

\paragraph{Response style shifts.}
Prompt framing alters response style indicators (Table~\ref{tab:prompt_response_styles}).
The Neutral prompt increased extreme responding from 23.2\% (Default) to 31.3\%, while the Persona prompt reduced it to 17.6\%.
Midpoint responding ranged from 43.7\% (Direct) to 51.9\% (Default).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We characterized systematic response style variation across 15 model families, 9 dimensions, 61 items, and 12 seeds per model, all administered via unified APIs.
Model families differ substantially in their response distributions (median pairwise $d = 1.01$), with Neuroticism showing the largest between-family effect ($\eta^2_p = 0.75$); however, architecture, training intensity, and provider are confounded, and we cannot attribute this variation to any single factor.
Exploratory structural analysis ($n = 15$) suggests the inter-dimension correlation structure reorganizes relative to humans: the Conscientiousness--Neuroticism correlation reverses from $r = -0.25$ to $r = +0.76$ (95\% CI $[0.47, 0.92]$), and the convergent validity test yields insufficient evidence for construct equivalence (3/5 sign agreement, $p = 0.50$); however, these structural claims require replication with a larger model sample.

These findings demonstrate that LLM psychometric responses capture systematic response style variation shaped by training and alignment, not human-like personality traits.
Critically, our prompt sensitivity experiment (Section~\ref{sec:prompt_sensitivity}) shows that surface-level changes to question framing produce score shifts (median $|d| = 0.49$--$0.98$) comparable to between-model differences, and prompt condition explains 16--32\% of total variance---direct evidence that these scores are prompt-dependent response styles rather than stable traits.
The observed correlation reorganization is consistent with shared alignment pressure toward socially desirable endpoints---a hypothesis supported by our aligned-vs.-base comparison (Section~\ref{sec:aligned_base}), where base models default to the Likert midpoint while aligned models show compressed but differentiated profiles; however, both the structural analysis ($n = 15$) and the aligned-vs.-base comparison (which confounds alignment with scale) are preliminary and require replication.
Following \citet{binz2023psychology}, we treat psychometric items as standardized stimuli that probe response tendencies, and our results reinforce this stance: the label ``Extraversion'' or ``Neuroticism'' applied to an LLM should not be assumed to denote the same construct it denotes in humans.
We recommend that the community treat LLM psychometric responses as measures of response style---systematic tendencies shaped by training data, alignment objectives, and deployment configurations---rather than as evidence for human-like personality.
Future work should extend the aligned-vs.-base paradigm to additional model families and develop evaluation frameworks specifically designed for LLM behavioral measurement rather than adapting human personality inventories.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations constrain interpretation.
First, our sample of model families is small ($n = 15$); Pearson correlations at this sample size yield wide confidence intervals, and the structural observations (correlation reversal, inconclusive convergent validity, exploratory PCA) should be treated as preliminary signals requiring replication with a broader model sample.
Second, only 2 of 15 models are closed-source (GPT-5, Claude Opus~4.5); findings may not generalize to the broader population of closed-source models, and the open-source--closed-source comparison (13 vs.\ 2) is underpowered.
Third, architecture, training intensity, and provider are partially confounded: we cannot attribute observed differences to any single factor, and the Dense--MoE comparison (10 vs.\ 5 families) does not reach significance.
Fourth, psychometric scales were designed for human self-report; the convergent validity result (3/5 sign agreement) provides insufficient evidence that the BFI dimension labels measure the same constructs in LLMs as in humans.
Fifth, although our aligned-vs.-base comparison (Section~\ref{sec:aligned_base}) provides preliminary evidence for alignment-driven response compression, it covers only 3 of 15 families and the Qwen comparisons confound alignment with scale (8B/14B $\to$ 397B); same-scale model pairs would provide cleaner causal evidence.
Sixth, this is a descriptive study---we quantify patterns without establishing causal mechanisms, and the alignment-based account we offer should be treated as one of several plausible explanations.
Seventh, all tested models are aligned/instruct versions accessible through commercial APIs; our aligned-vs.-base comparison (Section~\ref{sec:aligned_base}) and thinking ablation (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_thinking}) provide complementary evidence for alignment effects, but the base models tested represent only a subset of families.
Eighth, within-family trajectory analyses have 2--6 data points per family and are descriptive.
Finally, our use of temperature $= 0.7$ introduces response variance that may not generalize to low-temperature deployments.
Our prompt sensitivity experiment (Section~\ref{sec:prompt_sensitivity}) tests only four framings; additional framings (e.g., system prompts, few-shot examples) may produce different patterns, and two models were unavailable for the Direct condition.

\section{Exploratory PCA Details}
\label{sec:appendix_pca}

We present the full exploratory PCA results here, with the caveat that PCA on $n = 15$ model families with 9 dimensions is severely underpowered and should be treated as hypothesis-generating.

\paragraph{Decomposition.}
We decompose the 9-dimension model-level correlation matrix ($n = 31$ models including within-family variants) via PCA:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{W}\mathbf{\Lambda}^{1/2}\mathbf{V}^\top
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{\Lambda} = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_9)$ and $\sum_{k} \lambda_k / \sum_j \lambda_j$ gives the proportion of variance explained.

\paragraph{Factor structure.}
PC1 (51.1\% variance) loads most strongly on HEXACO-H ($+0.82$), with secondary loadings on Intuition ($+0.31$) and Conscientiousness ($+0.29$).
PC2 (14.0\% variance) isolates Neuroticism ($+0.52$) and HEXACO-H ($-0.51$).
PC3 (11.0\% variance) loads most strongly on Collectivism ($-0.71$).
These three factors explain 76.1\% of variance, compared to the five BFI factors expected in humans.
The factor structure does not cleanly recover the expected Big Five organization, aligning with the inter-dimension correlation divergences reported in \S\ref{sec:interdim}.

\paragraph{Extraversion reversal.}
Extraversion is significantly negatively correlated with Neuroticism ($r = -0.65$, $p = 0.009$) and Openness ($r = -0.60$, $p = 0.019$), with moderate negative correlations with HEXACO-H ($r = -0.48$, $p = 0.069$) and Conscientiousness ($r = -0.38$, $p = 0.161$) that do not reach significance.
In humans, Extraversion is positively correlated with Openness; in LLMs the sign flips.
Models that score high on Extraversion tend to be emotionally stable but closed-minded---a pattern with no direct human personality analog.

\paragraph{Agreeableness.}
Agreeableness shows a significant positive correlation with Conscientiousness ($r = +0.56$, $p = 0.029$) and a borderline correlation with Openness ($r = +0.50$, $p = 0.058$), but has no significant correlation with Collectivism ($r = -0.02$).
In humans, Agreeableness correlates moderately with Conscientiousness ($r = +0.20$) and weakly with Openness ($r = +0.10$); in LLMs, the A--C connection is substantially stronger.

\paragraph{Interpretation caution.}
Whether the observed factor structure generalizes to larger and more diverse model samples remains an open question.
As a rule of thumb, factor analysis typically requires at least 5--10 observations per variable; with $n = 15$ families and 9 dimensions, we fall below even the most lenient recommendation.
These results should be treated as suggestive patterns that motivate future investigation, not as evidence for a specific factor structure.

\section{Inter-Dimension Structure and Convergent Validity (Detailed)}
\label{sec:appendix_structure}

The main text (\S\ref{sec:interdim}) summarizes the key structural findings. Here we provide the full analysis.

\subsection{Correlation Matrix}
The 9$\times$9 model-level correlation matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:inter_dim_corr}) suggests that the BFI five-factor structure does not recover in our sample of LLMs.
Most notably, the Conscientiousness--Neuroticism correlation is strongly positive ($r = +0.76$, 95\% CI $[0.47, 0.92]$, $p = 0.001$), the reverse of the human baseline ($r = -0.25$).
This reversal holds across several subgroups: MoE ($r = +0.66$, $p = 0.05$), small models ($r = +0.83$, $p = 0.04$), and open-source ($r = +0.70$, $p = 0.007$), though it is non-significant for the small Dense subset ($p = 0.44$, $n = 4$).

\paragraph{Tentative explanation: alignment-driven compression.}
In humans, Conscientiousness and Neuroticism are weakly negatively correlated because they tap independent factors \citep{john1999bigfive}.
In LLMs, both dimensions may be compressed toward socially desirable endpoints by safety alignment, inducing a positive correlation.
This is consistent with the HEXACO-H results (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_hexaco_detail}), though the small sample ($n = 15$) means alternative explanations cannot be ruled out.

Beyond the C--N reversal, Extraversion is negatively correlated with Openness ($r = -0.60$, 95\% CI $[-0.81, -0.20]$, $p = 0.019$), and Agreeableness shows a stronger correlation with Conscientiousness ($r = +0.56$, 95\% CI $[0.26, 0.84]$, $p = 0.029$) than in humans ($r = +0.20$).

\subsection{Convergent Validity}
We compare LLM inter-dimension correlations against published human baselines \citep{john1999bigfive, oconnor2007bfi} for 5 key dimension pairs (Table~\ref{tab:convergent_validity}).
Three of five pairs agree in sign, including Extraversion--Neuroticism (human $r = -0.30$, LLM $r = -0.65$, 95\% CI $[-0.86, -0.27]$) and Agreeableness--Conscientiousness (human $r = +0.20$, LLM $r = +0.56$, 95\% CI $[0.26, 0.84]$).
Two pairs disagree: Extraversion--Agreeableness flips from positive to negative, and Conscientiousness--Neuroticism reverses to $r = +0.76$ (95\% CI $[0.47, 0.92]$).
The 60\% sign agreement rate does not exceed chance (binomial $p = 0.50$).
This test is severely underpowered at $n = 15$, but the systematic nature of the C--N divergence provides a preliminary signal warranting further investigation.

\section{HEXACO-H: Detailed Architecture and Scale Analysis}
\label{sec:appendix_hexaco_detail}

\textbf{Note:} HEXACO-H has unacceptably low internal consistency ($\alpha = 0.27$, 5 items). The analyses below are provided for completeness but should be interpreted with extreme caution.

Dense models average $H = 1.91$ vs.\ MoE models $H = 1.31$ ($d = 0.91$, 95\% CI $[0.66, 1.20]$, $p < 0.001$; Table~\ref{tab:architecture_effects}), with 10 Dense and 5 MoE families.
However, the Dense and MoE groups are not balanced across providers---all MoE models are from Chinese providers, while Dense models include GPT and Claude---so architecture and provider effects cannot be disentangled.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{figures/fig6_hexaco_h_params.pdf}
    \caption{HEXACO-H vs.\ total parameters across all tested models (27 with disclosed sizes + 4 undisclosed). Colors indicate architecture type. The architecture split (Dense vs.\ MoE) explains more variance than the scale trend. $\alpha = 0.27$; interpret with caution.}
    \label{fig:hexaco_h_params}
\end{figure}

In the broader 27-model sample, H correlates negatively with log-transformed parameters ($r = -0.49$, $p = 0.009$; Figure~\ref{fig:hexaco_h_params}), indicating that larger models score lower---a pattern consistent with more intensive safety alignment at scale.
H's high $\sd$ may be partially amplified by floor effects (7/15 models score at or below 1.15), but the Dense--MoE ranking is stable across all tested thresholds.
To jointly assess the effects of scale and architecture, we regress dimension scores on log-transformed parameters and architecture type across the 13 models with disclosed sizes (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_regression}).
HEXACO-H shows the highest explained variance ($R^2 = 0.41$), driven primarily by architecture, while Neuroticism shows the largest improvement from adding scale ($\Delta R^2 = +0.20$); however, no effect survives FDR correction at this sample size.
As partial evidence for alignment-driven compression, a thinking mode ablation (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_thinking}) across 4 models shows that reasoning mode further reduces HEXACO-H to the theoretical floor (1.00) and consistently decreases Conscientiousness and Neuroticism, patterns consistent with amplified alignment pressure during extended reasoning.
