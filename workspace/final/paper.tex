\begin{abstract}
When human-validated personality questionnaires are administered to large language models (LLMs), the resulting responses fail basic psychometric quality checks. We administered a 221-item battery (IPIP-NEO-120 Big Five, SD3 Dark Triad, ZKPQ-50-CC, EPQR-A) to 18 LLMs across 17 role-conditioned prompts (default + 16 MBTI personas), collecting 67,626 item-level responses at temperature = 0.7. We report four findings: (1) Cronbach's alpha for IPIP domains ranges from $-0.02$ to $0.36$ (vs.\ $0.87$--$0.90$ in human norms), indicating poor internal consistency. (2) Exploratory factor analysis recovers 3 factors instead of the expected 5+ (robust across leave-one-model-out and leave-one-persona-out). (3) 58.4\% [95\% CI: 53.5\%, 63.6\%] of forward-reverse item pairs show inconsistent responses, strongly associated with acquiescence bias ($r = 0.726$). (4) Inter-model variance accounts for less than 1\% of response variability. These results show that human personality instruments do not transport cleanly to LLMs. Response patterns are better explained by acquiescence-driven response style than by genuine trait-like structure.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The question of whether large language models (LLMs) possess ``personality'' has moved from philosophical speculation to empirical investigation. A growing body of work has administered human personality questionnaires---the Big Five Inventory, the Dark Triad scales, and others---to LLMs, reporting personality profiles that appear coherent on the surface \citep{miotto2023gpt3, bhandari2025evaluating, serapio2025psychometric, sorokovikova2024evaluating, lee2025trait}. These studies typically present domain-level scores, rank models along trait dimensions, and conclude that LLMs exhibit measurable personality differences. \citet{horton2023homo} proposed ``silicon samples'' as a framework for using LLMs as simulated survey respondents, further encouraging this line of work.

However, this research program rests on an untested assumption: that human-validated psychometric instruments retain their measurement properties when applied to non-human respondents. In personality psychology, an instrument is only valid for a population if it demonstrates adequate internal consistency (Cronbach's $\alpha$), recovers the expected factor structure, shows convergent validity with theoretically related constructs, and passes measurement invariance tests across subgroups \citep{cronbach1951coefficient, lord1968statistical}. None of these checks are routinely performed before interpreting LLM personality scores.

Recent work has begun to surface cracks. \citet{suhr2025challenging} showed that LLMs frequently endorse both forward-coded and reverse-coded items simultaneously, violating basic internal consistency. \citet{acerbi2024llm} and \citet{salecha2024llm} independently demonstrated that LLMs shift their responses toward socially desirable endpoints by up to 1.20 human standard deviations. \citet{pelet2025persistent} found that LLM personality measurements are persistently unstable across question reorderings, model scales, and conversation histories. \citet{huang2024reliability} tested the reliability of psychological scales on LLMs by varying instructions, language, and item format, finding that scale reliability is highly sensitive to these factors. \citet{dominguez2024questioning} critically examined LLM survey responses using the American Community Survey, showing that aggregate statistics lack the signals found in human populations. Together, these findings suggest that LLM personality scores may reflect response artifacts rather than genuine trait variation.

We take a different approach: rather than profiling LLM personality, we ask whether the instruments themselves are valid for this population. We administer a 221-item psychometric battery---comprising the IPIP-NEO-120 \citep{johnson2014measuring}, the Short Dark Triad \citep{jones2014introducing}, the ZKPQ-50-CC \citep{aluja2006cross}, and the EPQR-A \citep{francis1992development}---to 18 LLMs across 17 role-conditioned prompts, and subject the resulting 67,626 responses to six standard psychometric validation analyses. Our central finding is that human personality instruments do not transport cleanly to LLMs: internal consistency collapses ($\alpha$ ranges from $-0.02$ to $0.36$, versus $0.87$--$0.90$ in human norms), the Big Five factor structure reduces from five to three factors, and inter-model variance accounts for less than 1\% of response variability.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Personality Measurement in LLMs}

The application of personality instruments to LLMs has grown rapidly. \citet{miotto2023gpt3} administered the Big Five Inventory (BFI) to GPT-3, reporting measurable personality profiles. \citet{serapio2025psychometric} developed a comprehensive psychometric framework for evaluating and shaping personality traits across 18 LLMs, finding that instruction-tuned models can produce seemingly reliable measurements under specific prompt conditions. \citet{bhandari2025evaluating} applied five personality measurement tools to multiple LLMs, reporting varying consistency across personality dimensions. \citet{sorokovikova2024evaluating} introduced the Machine Personality Inventory and a chain prompting method for standardized evaluation. \citet{lee2025trait} proposed TRAIT, a psychometrically-grounded personality testset specifically designed for LLMs. \citet{hagendorff2024machine} investigated whether LLMs can develop dark personality profiles through fine-tuning on psychometric datasets.

However, these studies predominantly report descriptive personality profiles without validating that the instruments measure coherent constructs in LLMs. The critical step---establishing measurement invariance between human and LLM populations---is typically assumed rather than tested \citep{suhr2025challenging}. \citet{treaux2025forced} compared forced-choice and Likert formats for LLM personality measurement, finding that forced-choice better captures inter-model differences and is less susceptible to response biases. \citet{chen2025humanizing} surveyed the broader landscape, noting that most existing frameworks lack rigorous psychometric validation.

\subsection{Response Style and Acquiescence Bias}

Survey methodology has long recognized that self-report data is contaminated by response styles---systematic tendencies to respond to items regardless of content \citep{lord1968statistical}. Acquiescence bias (the tendency to agree with statements regardless of direction) is particularly damaging for instruments that use reverse-coded items as an internal consistency check. \citet{acerbi2024llm} and \citet{salecha2024llm} independently demonstrated that LLMs exhibit human-like social desirability biases in Big Five surveys, shifting scores toward socially desirable endpoints. Critically, \citet{acerbi2024llm} found this bias persists even after item randomization and rephrasing, ruling out pure acquiescence as the sole mechanism. Recent work has directly investigated acquiescence bias in LLMs across multiple languages \citep{acquiescence2025emnlp}, confirming the phenomenon is cross-linguistically robust. This suggests that LLM response contamination is multi-determined, combining acquiescence with social desirability and potentially alignment-driven prosociality.

\subsection{Alignment Effects on LLM Behavior}

RLHF and safety alignment training fundamentally shape LLM behavior. \citet{ouyang2022training} showed that reinforcement learning from human feedback produces models that are more helpful and harmless, but potentially less diverse in their outputs. \citet{bai2022constitutional} introduced Constitutional AI as an approach to training safer models through principle-guided self-correction. \citet{sharma2024sycophancy} documented sycophantic behavior in LLMs---the tendency to echo user opinions even when wrong---showing RLHF-trained models are significantly more sycophantic than base models. \citet{zheng2024agreeableness} linked Big Five Agreeableness to sycophancy in role-playing LLMs, showing it functions as a reliable predictor of persona-induced sycophancy. \citet{wang2024aligning} investigated how RLHF encodes a specific notion of acceptability that may override genuine variation. These alignment procedures may create a universal ``moderate prosocial'' response profile that overrides genuine variation across models and training regimes.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Psychometric Instruments}

We administered a 221-item battery comprising four validated instruments (\cref{tab:instruments}). The \textbf{IPIP-NEO-120} \citep{johnson2014measuring} contains 120 Likert-5 items measuring the Big Five domains through 6 facets each, with 41 reverse-coded items. The \textbf{SD3} \citep{jones2014introducing} contains 27 Likert-5 items measuring Machiavellianism, Narcissism, and Psychopathy, with 5 reverse-coded items. The \textbf{ZKPQ-50-CC} \citep{aluja2006cross} contains 50 True/False items measuring 5 traits, with 12 reverse-coded items. The \textbf{EPQR-A} \citep{francis1992development} contains 24 Yes/No items measuring 4 traits, with 5 reverse-coded items.

Each model was tested under 17 conditions: a \textbf{Default} prompt (``Please answer the following personality questionnaire honestly.'') and 16 \textbf{MBTI persona} prompts, each assigning a specific MBTI type. All administrations used temperature = 0.7 with a single administration per model-condition pair. This yielded $18 \times 17 \times 221 = 67{,}626$ item-level responses.

\subsection{Analysis Pipeline}

We applied six psychometric validation analyses:

\textbf{(1) Internal consistency.} Cronbach's $\alpha$ per IPIP domain, computed per model across 17 personas and then averaged. Human benchmarks from \citet{johnson2014measuring}.

\textbf{(2) Exploratory factor analysis.} Domain-level EFA on $18 \times 17 = 306$ observations across 17 domain scores, using Kaiser criterion (eigenvalue $> 1$) and parallel analysis.

\textbf{(3) Pairwise Inconsistency Rate (PIR).} For each forward-reverse item pair, classified as ``inconsistent'' if both responses fell on the same side of the midpoint.

\textbf{(4) Variance decomposition.} Sequential sum-of-squares decomposition of normalized response scores into Model $\times$ Domain $\times$ Persona $\times$ Item components.

\textbf{(5) Convergent validity.} Pearson and Spearman correlations between theoretically overlapping constructs across instruments.

\textbf{(6) Measurement invariance.} Pearson correlation between each model's Default persona item-level profile and each MBTI persona profile.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The Alignment Personality Hypothesis}

Our findings form a coherent picture of what we term the ``alignment personality''---a response pattern imposed by safety training rather than genuine trait variation:

\textbf{(1) Factor collapse.} The Big Five collapses to three factors because Neuroticism and Agreeableness share a single ``socially desirable emotional stability'' dimension. This aligns with \citet{sharma2024sycophancy}'s finding that RLHF produces sycophantic behavior, and \citet{wang2024aligning}'s observation that alignment encodes a specific notion of acceptability.

\textbf{(2) Acquiescence as mechanism.} The $r = 0.726$ correlation between reverse-item agreement and PIR identifies acquiescence as the specific contaminant. This extends \citet{acquiescence2025emnlp}'s cross-linguistic finding by quantifying the mechanism at per-model granularity across 18 LLMs.

\textbf{(3) Negligible model variance.} The 0.34\% inter-model variance means all aligned LLMs converge on a similar ``moderate prosocial'' profile regardless of architecture or training regime, consistent with \citet{zheng2024agreeableness}'s link between Agreeableness and sycophancy.

\textbf{(4) Persona override.} Persona injection improves consistency (PIR agreement 0.667 $\to$ 0.830), suggesting models can be consistent within assigned roles. This resonates with \citet{treaux2025forced}'s finding that response format mediates bias.

\subsection{Implications for LLM Personality Research}

Our results carry three implications. First, studies administering personality instruments to LLMs \emph{must} report internal consistency, factor structure, and convergent validity before interpreting scores \citep{huang2024reliability}. Second, researchers should establish measurement invariance before comparing personality across conditions. Third, multi-temperature and multi-seed designs are needed to disentangle stochastic variance from genuine trait variance.

\section{Limitations}

Our study has four main limitations. First, we use a \textbf{single administration} at temperature 0.7, preventing separation of stochastic variance from genuine trait variance. Second, we lack \textbf{base vs.\ instruct model comparisons}, so we cannot isolate alignment effects. Third, we have \textbf{no multi-seed data}. Fourth, we compare against \textbf{published human norms} rather than running human participants.

\section{Conclusion}

We have shown that four validated personality instruments fail basic psychometric quality checks when administered to 18 LLMs: Cronbach's alpha collapses from $0.87$--$0.90$ to $-0.02$--$0.36$, the Big Five factor structure collapses from five to three factors, 58.4\% of forward-reverse pairs show inconsistent responses, and inter-model variance accounts for less than 1\% of variability. These failures are robust across leave-one-out analyses. Validated human psychometric instruments do not transport cleanly to LLMs---researchers must first establish measurement invariance and account for response-style contamination.

\section*{Ethics Statement}

This study uses publicly available LLMs and public-domain psychometric instruments. No human subjects were involved. We do not claim that LLMs have or lack genuine personality; our contribution concerns the \emph{measurement properties} of the instruments when applied to a novel population.
